1.   Introduction

 

In my last blog on Fatigue Failure [https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/fatigue-failure-part-6-randomly-varying-stresses-miners-ajay-taneja/], I had been speaking about “Randomly Varying Stresses”, the Miner’s Rule. It may be recalled that Miner’s rule is a Linear Cumulative Damage Rule for a component / part (precisely, feature of a part) being subjected to repeated variable amplitude cycles of loading as illustrated in the figure below;

Figure 1: Application of Miner’s Rule for a feature of a part being subjected to variable amplitude cycles of loading

Using the Miner’s Rule, it was possible to calculate the “damage” induced during each cycle of loading. Having calculated the damage, it was possible to calculate the total life considering that the cycles are repeated over and over again.

That is; considering the stress cycles shown in the Figure 1, it was quite simple to calculate the number of cycles subjected to a particular stress range – the distinct cycles being shown in red, green and purple colour in the Figure 1 above. However, this might most often not be the case. That is; the loading history might at many times be complicated as shown in the Figure below.

Figure 2: Loading History

In such cases (Figure 2), it might generally not be possible to detect the number of cycles corresponding to a particular stress range as distinctly as in Figure 1.

Several methods have been proposed for general cycle counting such as: Rainflow cycle counting technique, Level Crossing, Reservoir Counting Method, etc. Out of many methods available for cycle counting, Rainflow Cycle Counting technique is often applied and it gives the most precise physical representation of the fatigue process.

This article attempts to gives an insight into the “Rainflow” cycle counting technique and the following aspects of this cycle counting method are elaborated in the subsequent sections;

·       Section 2 explains in detail of the relationship of the “Rainflow” cycle counting technique to uniaxial plasticity

·       Section 3 talks of the From-To Rainflow Cycle Matrix in terms of Stress Range

·       Section 4 details about the formulating a Rainflow Cycle Counting Algorithm

2.   Relationship of the “Rainflow” Cycle Counting Technique to Uniaxial Plasticity

 Through the content of the introductory section and my earlier blogs on Fatigue Failure (especially Part 6 of the series: https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/fatigue-failure-part-6-randomly-varying-stresses-miners-ajay-taneja/, that each “cycle” is a “damaging” event. Fatigue arises on account of the stress reversals that occur during a time history. The “Rainflow” Cycle Counting method is designed to count the stress reversals in accordance with the stress-strain relationship of the material including the hysteresis loop. Rainflow cycle counting is in-fact the only technique that identifies and accounts for all the cycles in a variable amplitude loading history.

The basic principle of the “Rainflow” Cycle Counting technique can be illustrated through the following examples;

4.1         Peak Picking:

In order to develop an algorithm for the rainflow cycle counting technique let us consider a variable amplitude loading as shown in the Figure below;

In order to develop an algorithm for the rainflow cycle counting technique let us consider a variable amplitude loading as shown in the Figure below;

Figure 21: Example Variable Amplitude Loading considered For Algorithm Development

Let us consider a block loading as shown so that we have a series of blocks that repeat one after another. The red colour points shown below represent the data points through which plot is generated.

Figure 22: Example Variable Amplitude Loading considered For Algorithm Development – loading being repeated in blocks


To make things simpler, what we do is that: we wrap the time history so that we start and end with th extreme value i.e. the highest peak.

Since, in the above example, we are considering a block that is repeated the extreme values are the same which means all the hysteresis loops will close. If we do not start and end with the extreme values, there will be a bunch of hysteresis loops that will be uncloses and will thus require special techniques to be dealt with in the algorithm. In the current example let us wrap the time history around the location starting and ending with the extreme value.

Next, it should be understood that, the analyst really wants only the peaks and valleys associated with the loading history. Thus, next, the data is reduced so that one gets only peaks and valleys in the time history as marked below. This process is called “Peak-Picking”

Thus, the points which we want to keep in the time history are marked below;

Figure 23: Example Variable Amplitude Loading considered For Algorithm Development – Peaks and Valleys marked in Circles

Given a data-set and In order to identify, whether we have a peak or a valley, the easiest thing to do is to take 3 consecutive points and check whether the slope between 2 consecutive points is the same sign or opposite sign. For example, let us assume that we stress – time history comprising of a set of discreet data points got from the digitalized history as shown below;


Thus, comparing slopes 1-2 and 2-3, then, if the slopes have the same sign, then the peak/valley has not been identified. If the slopes have “different” signs then a “peak” or a “valley” has been identified. It is possible to process the entire time history in this way and it is possible to get a series of “peaks” and “valleys”.


Thus, as the first steps in developing a cycle counting algorithm we have;


a)    Consider the cycle between two consecutive peaks in a block

5.   Summary of the Algorithmic Ingredients of “Rainflow” Cycle Counting Technique

 

The basis of formulation of the “Rainflow Cycle Counting” algorithm has been discussed in the preceding sections. An outline of the step-by-step algorithm is provided below;

1.     Firstly we need to pick successive peaks and eliminate the history prior to the peaks.

2.     Next, rearrange the time-history comprising of peaks and valleys only.

3.     Store all the points of the modified cycle in an array (or, as appropriate)

4.     Pick the first three consecutive points from the stored points

5.     Let’s say and call the points as A,B and C

6.     There are three possibilities involving the Range AB and Range BC as explained in section 4.2. The three possibilities being;

a)    Range BC = Range AB

b)    Range BC > Range AB

c)     Range BC < Range AB

7.     Depending upon wheteher one encounters a) or b) or c) proceed as explained in the example in section 4.3
