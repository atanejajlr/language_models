1:   Introduction:

 

This article is the continuation of my series of blogs on “Fracture Mechanics” and is perhaps the second last in the series.

So far the discussion in my earlier blogs was resorted to linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) wherein the focus was on limited plasticity at the crack tip. Using the concepts developed in linear elastic fracture mechanics, a variety of problems have been solved in the industry. In fatigue problems wherein the applied loads are generally about 30% of the yield stress of the material and high strength alloys are normally resorted to, the concepts involving linear elastic fracture mechanics proved an ideal choice because of limited plasticity at the crack tip.

However, there could be situations wherein the analysis based on linear elastic fracture mechanics is inappropriate in cases where there is significant plastic zone developed at the crack tip. In such situations, one has to look at approaches other than linear elastic fracture mechanics. A direct approach involving rigorous plasticity theory wherein one has to keep a track of the loading history throughout the crack propagation analysis is often not very convenient because of the computational cost and numerical problems. And in such cases approaches based on linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) are modified and quite frequently adapted to elasto-plastic fracture mechanics (EPFM).

With regards to EPFM, there are 2 fundamental concepts that should be understood in some detail;

o  J – integral

o  Crack tip opening displacement (CTOD)

This article focusses on the J – integral and subsequent version of this article will elaborate on CTOD. The section 2 of this article provides the mathematical as well as the physical interpretation of the J – integral

Section 3 of this article elaborates on the most fundamental and important mathematic property of the J – integral i.e. the path independence of the J – integral.

Section 4 provides a mathematical example leading to the computation of the J – integral through a hand calculation.

Section 5 explains the graphical interpretation of the J – integral in order to get a better physical insight

Section 6 is on the application of J – integral to non-linear elastic and elastoplastic solids.

This article will mainly focus on the interpretation of the J – integral as the energy release rate (“G” in case of linear elastic fracture mechanics) whereas the next article will focus on its interpretation as stress intensity factor.

This actually proves a very important result: if one takes the integral from one crack surface and ends with the other crack surface, the value of the integral is independent of the path that is taken. This property of the path independence of the J – integral is well utilized in linear elasticity.

In the current scenario, engineers using finite element calculations in a software identify the path and the software churns out a number indicating the computed value of the J – integral. The physics and the quantities that go in the computation of J – integral is often not fully understood.

Conclusion: what is so special of J – integral?

This leads to a very important conclusion that in case of a linear elasticity problem, J and G are identical and since there is an inter-relationship between G and K, there exists an inter-relationship between J and K as well. During a finite element analysis, in many instances, one evaluates the line integral and from that computes the stress intensity factor.

And since the integral can be taken at a distance, somewhat remote from the crack tip (being path independent), a precise computation of the stress field in the crack tip region is unnecessary. That is to say, in a practical problem involving crack propagation analysis, if one is able to avoid the requirement of a precise mesh closer to the crack tip, there can result in enormous amount of saving from the computational cost point of view. And it is here that the path independence property of J – integral proves very effective.

It may be recalled through the undergraduate course on “Applied Mechanics” that a non-linear elastic behaviour signifies that the solid deforms reversibly; if the loads on the solid are removed, then, it returns to the original shape i.e. the unloading path will trace back the same way as the unloading path as shown in the figure below;

Whereas, in an elasto-plastic material, once a critical stress is exceeded, the material deforms permanently and does not regain its original shape.

Extension of J – integral for elasto - plastic fracture mechanics

As stated above, in case of linear elastic fracture mechanics and non – linear elastic fracture mechanics, the value of J is the same as G. From the graphical interpretation of J, it was made clear that considering the load deformation curve of a specimen with crack length “a” and another with crack length “a + δa”, the difference between the two (the shaded portion) indicates the energy availability to form 2 new surfaces.

In case of an elastoplastic material, the behaviour of the material during unloading is not the same as that during loading and some energy is lost during plastic deformation. An approximation is often used in elastoplastic analysis to allow for the use of J – integral wherein it is assumed that no unloading or redistribution of forces occurs.

Thus, the mathematical equations involving J – integral are valid for a non-linear elastic material but not completely for an elasto-plastic material because: for an elasto-plastic material the unloading path is different and there exists residual strain following the unloading and also the equations involving J – integral do not account for the redistribution of forces – which is bound to occur once the crack starts growing. It should be underscored that the mathematical expressions involving J – integral are able to predict large plastic zone around the crack tip unlike linear elastic fracture mechanics which accounts only for limited plasticity at the crack tip.

However, from the designer’s perspective, the designer is most often interested in knowing the condition for the “onset of crack growth” – this requirement is exploited when the J – integral is applied to elasto-plastic materials. Because, if the crack growth is not accounted for in the calculations i.e. one accounts for only the initiation of crack then elasto-plastic behaviour is no longer different from the linear elastic behaviour as far as the mathematical equations are concerned. Thus, from the designer’s point of view, it is enough to estimate the load at which crack is about to propagate which is done considerable accuracy through the equations involving the J – integral.
