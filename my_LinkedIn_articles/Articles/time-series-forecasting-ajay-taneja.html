1.   What is Time Series Forecasting?

 To put it simply, time series forecasting is the use of statistical models to predict future values based on past results. Any variables that can be tracked and collected over time can be forecast using time series analysis. These may include:

  • Annual population data
  • A company’s daily stock price
  • OR a quarterly sales figures of some sales item

 For all of these variables, the data is collected over time and the time series model is simply using the data to forecast future values. Another problem from the structural mechanics point of view is to predict dependent variables given different performance cycles during the flight for an aircraft engine corresponding to take off, climb, cruise, turning, gliding, landing. This might be stated as an example of multivariate time series as shown in the table below:

Table: A time series problem from the Structural Mechanics point of view

Figure: Time Series from Structural Mechanics point of view – predicting the state variables of a section of a aircraft engine during different flight stages

The time series will need a numerical variable that we’d like to predict – this is our direct variable, and it also needs a time-based variable. In the examples considered above: year over year population, a stocks daily closing price and quarterly sales figure are all numeric variables. The data for these examples are collected by year, day and quarter respectively. In case of the structural mechanics problem considered above the time-based variable includes the state variable displacement. Each observation in the problem has a time component to it – if the business problem involves predicting these values into the future, then time series forecasting models like ARIMA or ETS models like Simple Exponential Smoothing, Holts Linear Trend Methods or Holtz Winter Seasonal Method or Facebooks Prophet or Neural Prophet.

2.1 Trend Component:

 The trend component of the time series represents a persistent, long-term change in the mean of the time series. The trend is the slowest moving part of the time series, the part representing the largest time scale of importance. In a timeseries of product scales, an increasing trend might be the effect of a market expansion as more people become aware of the product year after year

Trend is the result of the influences such as population growth, price inflation, general economic change. The following graph predicts a series where there is an obvious uptrend over time.

Moving Average Plots:

To see what kind of a trend a time series might have, we can use a moving average plot. To compute the moving average of a time series we compute the average of the values within a sliding window of some defined width. Each point on the graph represents the average of all the values in the series that fall within the window on either side. The idea of Moving Average is to smooth out any short-term fluctuations in the series so that only long-term changes remain.

Uptrend and Downtrend:

When trend pattern exhibits a general direction that is upwards where there are higher highs and higher lows, we call this an uptrend. Whereas, when the trend pattern exhibits a general direction is down where there are lower highs and lower lows, we call this a downtrend. A time series can experience changing direction where it can go from an Uptrend to a Downtrend. If there were no trend we call it a horizontal or stationary trend. Each trend we see in the time series we call it as a “Trend Cycle”

Figure: Uptrend – Downtrend – Horizontal Trend

 

 2.2  Seasonality

 We say that a time series exhibits seasonality whenever there is a regular, periodic change in the mean of the series. Seasonal changes generally follow clock and calendar – repetitions over a day, over a year are some examples. Seasonality is often driven by cycles of the natural world over days and years or by conventions of social behaviour surrounding dates and times.

In the structural mechanics example, one can observe the stresses during take-off, climb, cruise, turning, gliding and sliding and observe the variations which will follow a “seasonal” pattern analogous to the temperature during the seasons – hence underscoring that the concept of seasonality might eb generic to many time series problems.

 

 Seasonal Plots and Seasonal Indicators

Just like we use a moving average plot to discover the trend in a time series, we use a “seasonal” plot to discover the seasonal patterns. A seasonal plot shows segments of the time series plotted against some common period - the period being the season you want to observe. 

Figure: Time Series Plots during Summer and Winter Months (Time Series corresponding to hotel months)

 2.3 Cyclic Patterns

 A cyclical pattern exists when the data exhibits rise and falls which are not of a fixed period. In finance, time of recession and expansion in the stock market reveals cyclical patterns. A cyclic uptrend is referred to as a “Bull-market” and a cyclical downtrend is often referred to as a “Bear-Market”. These patterns in the market occupy multiple years and do not have repeating pattern each year.

It should be underscored that there is a difference between a cyclical behaviour and a seasonal behaviour. If the fluctuations are not of a fixed period, then, they are cyclical. If the period is unchanging and associated with some aspect of the calendar then the pattern is seasonal.

4.   Predictive ETS Model Types in Time Series

Let us now look at the a few predictive models for time series forecasting – starting from the simplest - the exponential smoothing models.

Exponential smoothing models use the weighted average of the past observations giving more weight to the most recent observation with weights getting smaller as observations get older.

The ETS term refers to how Error, Trend and Seasonality are applied in the smoothing method calculation – each term can be applied additively or multiplicatively, or it may be left out of the model completely. This framework (of ETS) allows for wide spectrum of time series analysis because of the simplicity of the calculation.


4.1 Time Series Decomposition


How do we determine how to apply the Error, Trend and Seasonality of an ETS model? One way is to look at the time series decomposition plot – the plot separates the time series into seasonal, trend and error components. The Trend and the Seasonal plots have been discussed in section 2. The Error component of the time series decomposition will show the portion of the time series that is not accounted for by combining the seasonal portion and the trend portion of the time series. Combining the Trend, Seasonality and Error component of the time series helps us identify the main component of the time series – we then extract the component to best represent the underlying data of the time series.

4.2 Additive or Multiplicative Behaviour in Time Series


Selecting whether a time series exhibits additive or multiplicative behaviour in its terms depends upon the analyst’s ability to see trend, seasonality and the error patterns. While visualizing the data may not be appear scientific, it is the most effective way to determine how to apply a model.

The additive method is useful when the trend and seasonal variation is relatively constant over time as shown below whereas the multiplicative method is useful when the trend and seasonal variation increases or decreases in magnitude over time

  • Does the Time Series have a Seasonality? IF yes, do the Seasonal Components increase in magnitude over time?

  • No Trend, Seasonality Constant
  • No Trend, Seasonality Increasing

  • Linear Trend, Seasonality Increasing

  • Exponential Trend, Seasonality Increasing

6.   Holtz Linear Trend Method


Holtz extended the simple exponential smoothing method to include forecasting data with Trend. The method is termed as “Double Exponential Smoothing” or “Holtz Linear Trend Method”. The method builds up on Simple Exponential Smoothing by including not only the level (smoothing) but also the Trend in the calculation.

It does this by combing 2 smoothing calculations: one for the level and the other for the trend. The trend in this case is always applied in the additive or linear fashion. By adding the trend component to the smoothing our forecast will no longer be flat but will be able to trend upward or downward. Holts linear method is a great model to apply to any non-seasonal data set.

8.   Damped Trend Methods


Both the Holtz Linear Trend method and exponential trend method prove quite effective for forecasting a time series without seasonality. Sometimes, however, the model can over forecast results since the forecast generated display a constant trendline extrapolating values in the future.

Damped Trend methods as the name implies are solutions that implement a damped parameter that dampens the trend line into a flat line sometime into the future and this can be applied both additively and multiplicatively. The Damping parameter is normally denoted by Φ. Models with small values of Φ assume that the trend line changes very slowly over time whereas models with large values of Φ assume that the tend line changes very rapidly.

Normally, if the data analyst is unsure if the damped trend model should be implemented, then, the damped model and the original trend model are run side by side and the forecasting errors are checked to see which method fits the time series better.

Mathematical equations for Damped Methods:

In conjunction with the smoothing parameters α and β* (with values between 0 and 1 as in Holtz method) the Damped Method also includes a damping parameter 0 < Φ < 1 as shown below:

If Φ = 1, the method is identical to Holtz Linear Trend method. For values between 0 and 1, Φ dampens the trend so that it approaches a constant time sometime in the future.

In practice, Φ is rarely less than 0.8 as the damping has a very strong effect for smaller values. Values of Φ close to 1 will mean that a damped model is not able to be distinguished from a non-damped model. For these reasons, we usually restrict Φ to a minimum of 0.8 and a maximum of 0.98.


9.   Holtz Winter Seasonal Method


Holtz Winter seasonal method accounts for all the three components that can be found in the time series. The Holtz Winter seasonal method comprises of the forecast equation with 3 smoothing equations: one for the level, one for the trend and one for the seasonality. Again, there can be two variations of the application in the method: additive and multiplicative

Re-stating the definitions of additive and multiplicative variations:

The additive method is used when the seasonal fluctuation does not change in magnitude over time and multiplicative method is used when seasonal fluctuations change in magnitude as shown in the figure below. Holts Winter seasonal method can also be used with a damping parameter and is one of the most widely regarded method for forecasting seasonal dates.
