1. INTRODUCTION

Response of a structure to earthquake excitation is generally obtained though one of the two approaches: a) Response history analysis or b) Response spectrum analysis.

The response history analysis (‘time history analysis’) determines the displacement of the structure as a function of time, given the ground acceleration (also a function of time.)  The ground acceleration   is available from seismic records.

The system is studied in a reference frame which moves with the ground.  In such a coordinate system, the ground appears stationary, and every mass feels an applied force .  This reduces the earthquake to a standard problem with loading:

Figure: Effective earthquake force

This procedure requires much computation, since it calculates the response of the structure in detail for each set of ground acceleration data.

On the other hand, the response spectrum analysis (RSA) is a procedure for dynamic analysis of a structure subjected to earthquake excitation, but it reduces to a series of static analysis. For each mode, a static analysis of the structure is carried out (the forces being evaluated based on the peak response obtained from the spectrum) which is multiplied by the spectral ordinate of the pseudo acceleration response An to obtain the peak modal response. Thus, the response spectrum analysis avoids any dynamic analysis being carried out.

However, RSA is still a dynamic analysis procedure as it used the vibration properties: natural frequencies, natural modes and modal damping ratios of the structure and the dynamic characteristics of the ground motion through the response spectrum.

It is just that, in the response spectrum analysis, the user (or the software) will not need to carry out any response history calculations as it would have already been done in developing the earthquake response spectrum which becomes an input for the response spectrum analysis.

The aim of this article is to discuss in detail the concepts involving the response spectrum analysis. This technical article is organized as follows;

Section 2 of the article discusses fundamentally the response spectrum concept, which includes: the definition of a response spectrum, the response spectrum types- the deformation response spectrum, pseudo-velocity response spectrum, pseudo acceleration response spectrum as well as the physical interpretation of these response spectra.

It is also explained in section 2, that, given the response spectrum, the equivalent static force can be arrived at and the internal forces in the structure be thus determined. The explanation in the section 2 is confined to SDOF systems

Discussion in section 3 is confined to MDOF systems and it is explained that why the response for MDOF systems is not exact (i.e. the same as the response obtained from time history analysis) unlike that of SDOF systems.

This is followed by a considerable detailed discussion on the modal combination rules: the absolute sum modal combination rule (ABSSUM), the square root of sum of squares modal combination rule (SRSS) and the complete quadratic combination (CQC) modal combination rule.

Section 5 includes a note on the interpretation of the response spectrum analysis based on the details presented in section 2 through 4 and section 6 lists the step by step procedure concerning the analysis of a structure through response spectrum analysis.

Finally, section 7 provides a numerical example of the analysis of a simple 2 storey frame through response spectrum analysis.

2. RESPONSE SPECTRUM CONCEPT: REVIEW

The response spectrum concept is a central concept in earthquake engineering which provides a convenient means to summarize the peak response of all possible SDOF systems to a particular component of ground motion. It provides a practical approach to apply the knowledge of structural dynamics to design of structures for earthquakes.

2.1   What is ‘response spectrum’?

A plot of the peak value of a response quantity as a function of the natural vibration period  of the system, or a related parameter such as circular frequency  or cyclic frequency , is called the response spectrum of the quantity. 

Each such plot is for SDOF systems having a fixed damping ratio ζ and several such plots for different values of ζ are included to cover the range of damping values encountered in actual structures.

2.2.4    Physical interpretation of the Response spectra types

Deformation response spectrum provides the peak deformation of a system. The pseudo-velocity response spectrum is related to the peak strain energy stored in the system whereas the pseudo acceleration response spectrum is related to the peak value of equivalent static force.

It may occur to the mind, that, since the units of the quantity ‘V’ defined with regards to the pseudo-velocity response spectrum are the same as that of velocity, why not, use the velocity  in its definition. The reason for this apparent by considering the derivation of the expression for the quantity (as illustrated again below);

Since strain energy is caused by deformation of stiff members, it is related to u, while  is a velocity, and so it is related to the kinetic energy of the shaking building.   However, it is strain energy which correlates with structural failure, and so this is what we want to compute.

Another way to interpret the pseudo-velocity is that the amount of strain energy stored in the system is equivalent to the amount of stress.

This is because the strain energy stored in the system and the strain energy is , integrated across the volume of the system. 

Since stress is proportional to strain, the strain energy is proportional to stress squared, and also proportional to strain squared and thus can be related to the stress.

The term pseudo-velocity is used only because the units are same as that of velocity.

3. RESPONSE SPECTRUM ANALYSIS FOR MDOF SYSTEMS

The explanation concerning the response spectrum concept in section 2 involved SDOF system only. It is clear through the description presented that in case of SDOF systems, the peak response can be directly determined directly from the response spectrum for the ground motion  without carrying out a response history (otherwise termed as time history) analysis. The procedure to do that involved: a) compute the stiffness characteristics of the system b) compute the mass characteristics c) Calculate the natural time period d) Evaluate the peak deformation from the response spectrum corresponding to the damping ratio and natural time period e) Evaluate the equivalent static force f) Carry out static analysis to determine the internal forces.

For MDOF systems, too, the peak response can be calculated from the response spectrum but the result is not exact: in the sense that it is not identical to the result that is obtained through response history analysis. However, the estimate obtained is accurate enough for structural design applications. This will be clear from the discussions that follow.

 4.   Modal Combination Rules

The question that needs to be answered is: how does one combine the peak modal responses rno  (n = 1, 2,.., N) to determine the peak response r0 of the total   response?

It will not be possible to determine the exact value of r from r because, in general, the modal responses r (t) attain their peaks at different time instants and the combined response attains its peaks at yet another time instant.

Approximations must be introduced in combining the peak modal responses determines from the earthquake response spectrum because no information is available in the response spectrum when the peak modal response will occur. 

Most frequently encountered modal combination rules are described below;

4.1    Absolute sum modal combination rule (ABSSUM)

The assumption that all the peaks will occur at the same time will provide an upper bound to the peak value of the total response;

This upper bound is usually too conservative. Therefore, this absolute sum (ABSSUM) is not popular in structural design applications.

4.2   Square-root-of-sum-of-squares (SRSS) modal combination rule:

The square-root-of-sum-of-squares (SRSS) rule for modal combination, developed in E. Rosenbleuth’s Ph.D. thesis, is;

The peak response in each mode is squared, the squared modal peaks are summed and the square root of the sum provides an estimate of the peak total response.

This modal combination rule provides excellent response estimates for structures with well-separated natural frequencies.

It should be noted that this limitation has not always been recognised in applying this rule to practical problems, and at times it has been misapplied to systems with closely spaced natural frequencies such as piping systems in nuclear power plants and multi-storey systems with unsymmetrical plan.

The above statement concerning closely spaced natural frequencies in unsymmetrical buildings may be interpreted as follows;

 For example, a building which occupies an entire block for the first few storeys, and then has a tower which rises for several more storeys, but which has a smaller floor area than the lower part of the building.  In this case, each part of the building will have a separate set of natural frequencies.  Even though each set may be evenly space, when both are considered together, some frequencies from one set may lie very near to some frequencies from the other set. 

For instance, if the frequencies of one portion of the building are (in HZ) 1, 5, 9, 13, 17, 21, while the frequencies of the other portion are 4.1, 7.1, 10.1, 13.1, 16.1, 19.1.  In each case, the frequencies are widely space (4 Hz apart for the first portion, 3 Hz apart for the second portion) but when both are considered together, the frequencies 13 and 13.1 Hz are very close together, hence the SRSS modal combination rule may not be obvious choice in such cases.

The above may be difficult to imagine at first because  in modal analysis all the degrees of freedom of the structure vibrate harmonically at the same frequency.

However, if a building is composed to two parts (base and tower) which have different mode frequencies when analyzed separately, then when analyzed as complete building, the mode frequencies of the whole building are related to those of the separate parts. 

The relation is not very simple, but the mode frequencies of the whole are approximately sums and differences of the mode frequencies of the parts.  This can cause some mode frequencies of the whole structure to lie very close together and hence SRSS might not be the choice concerning the modal combination rule..

4.4  Assumptions underlying the SRSS and the CQC rules:

It is important to understand the implications of the assumptions behind the derivations of the SRSS and the CQC rules which are based on random vibration theory;

i . The assumptions indicate that the modal combination rules would be most accurate for earthquake excitations that contain a wide band of frequencies with long phases of strong shaking which are several times longer than the fundamental periods of the structures which are not too lightly damped. In particular, the modal combination rules will become less accurate for short duration impulsive ground motions.

It may be noted that the seismic excitations may have frequency spectra from less than 1Hz up to around 20Hz or so, and the excitation may last for up to 60 seconds.  This means that the time over which the excitation acts is long compared to the period of the excitation, since these have periods of between 1/20 second and 1 second.

The point that is being tried to convey above is that, the earthquake lasts long enough to drive the structure through several complete cycles of oscillation, which means that the fundamental modes have had time to reach their equilibrium level of dynamic excitation (the oscillation amplitude at which energy input from the excitation is balanced against energy loss from damping). 

If the duration of the earthquake were too short, then the modes would not have time to reach their full excitation (i.e. the damping would not have been sufficiently active as in an impulse loading), and so the estimates given by the SRSS and CQC modal rules would be wrong.

ii. Considering the SRSS and the CQC modal combination rules are based on the random vibration theory, r0 may be interpreted as the mean of the peak values of response to an ensemble of earthquake excitations. Thus, the modal combination rules are intended for use when the excitation is characterized by a smooth response spectrum based on the response spectrum of many earthquake excitations. Thus, the smooth spectrum may be the mean or the median of the individual response spectra or it be a more conservative response spectrum such as mean plus one standard deviation spectrum.

 iii. The CQC or the SRSS modal combination rule when used in conjunction with, say, the mean spectrum provides an estimate of the peak response that is reasonably close to the mean of the peak values of response to individual excitations.

5. Interpretation of Response Spectrum Analysis

As it may be observed from the preceding sections, the response spectrum analysis (RSA) is a procedure for dynamic analysis of a structure subjected to earthquake excitation, but it reduces to a series of static analyses.

For each mode considered, static analyses of the structure subjected to forces  provides the modal static response , which is multiplied by the spectral ordinate  to obtain the peak modal responses  ( ).

Thus, the RSA procedure avoids the dynamic analysis of the SDOF systems necessary for the response history analysis. However, the response spectrum analysis is still a dynamic analysis procedure because it uses the vibration properties: natural modes, natural frequencies and modal damping ratios of the structure and the dynamic characteristics of the ground motion through its response spectrum.

It is just that the user (or the software) does not have to carry out any response history calculations; as that would have already been done in developing the earthquake response spectrum.
