1.   Introduction

 Thermomechanical fatigue is the primary life limiting factor for engineering components in many high temperature applications. In early work, isothermal fatigue tests performed at various temperatures, mechanical strain ranges and strain rates were used to estimate life of members undergoing thermomechanical fatigue damage. Since, few years thermomechanical fatigue tests have been developed to simulate behaviour of material undergoing thermomechanical fatigue and it has thus been possible to develop TMF models that predict behaviour of materials under this type of loading. Some of these models are discussed in section 5 of this article.

 Section 2 and section 3 bring out some differences between isothermal and thermomechanical fatigue processes and explain the concept of In-Phase and Out-of-Phase effects. Section 4 of this article provides a background of different mechanisms that play a role in quantifying thermomechanical fatigue damage.

2.   Isothermal vs. Thermomechanical fatigue

 One of the major differences between isothermal and thermo-mechanical fatigue is the constraint. When heated, structures develop thermal gradients and they expand. Expansion is often not uniform and is constrained by surrounding cooler material. This results in mechanical strain causing stresses. These additional stresses due to thermal effects cause additional fatigue damage in structures.

 

3.   In-Phase thermomechanical fatigue loading and out-of-phase thermomechanical loading

 Thermomechanical fatigue loading is often described as out-of-phase (OP) or in-phase loading (IP). In out-of-phase loading, the material experiences compression at higher temperature and tension at low temperature. The inverse is observed for thermomechanical fatigue in-phase case. 4.   Thermomechanical Fatigue mechanisms

 

Thermomechanical fatigue is caused by combined thermal and mechanical loading where both stress and temperature vary with time. This type of loading can be more damaging than an order of magnitude by isothermal fatigue (where cyclically varying load is imposed at constant temperature).

 Various mechanisms play a role in the type of damage formed in a material due to thermomechanical fatigue. These include;

·       Material properties

·       Mechanical strain range

·       Strain Rate

·       Temperature

·       Phasing between temperature and mechanical strain

Thermomechanical damage mechanism prevalent in metals involves 3 major aspects;

a)    Fatigue Damage

b)    Environmental Damage (Oxidation)

c)     Creep Damage

Fatigue Damage:

Thermomechanical fatigue damage is time and temperature independent damage that exists whenever cyclic loading occurs. Fatigue damage may be cyclic plasticity driven.

Creep Damage:

Under thermomechanical fatigue loading, creep deformation contributes to formation and propagation of micro-cracks [https://www.corrosionpedia.com/definition/5709/microcrack]. Maximum stress rather than strain range has a dominant role in the formation of these micro-cracks. The interaction of strain rate and temperature has a strong influence on the stresses that are observed during cyclic loading.

Environmental Damage (Oxidation):

 In out-of-phase loading, an oxide layer can form when the material is hot and under compression. At lower temperatures (in tension), the oxide layer becomes brittle, cracks and thus exposing the new clean metal surfaces. This clean metal rapidly oxidizes and the process repeats during the next mechanical strain cycle.

Oxide cracks can also form during In-Phase loading – the oxide layer can form in the hot portion of the material in tension and cracks as the material cools. Oxide formation occurs easier and faster at higher temperatures. The tendency of the oxide to develop micro-cracks depends on the cyclic stress strain range and the stress level.
