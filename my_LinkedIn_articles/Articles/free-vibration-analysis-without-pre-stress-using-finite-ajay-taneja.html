1. INTRODUCTION

 

A free vibration analysis involves (ultimately) the computation of the natural frequencies and mode shapes of the structure through the solution of the equation;

The computation of the natural frequencies and their comparison to the forcing frequencies aids the engineer to establish whether the natural frequencies are far away from the forcing frequencies, thus eliminating the chance of resonance. If the natural frequencies computed through the free vibration analysis procedure are closer to the forcing frequencies, a redesign of the system is normally carried out and the free vibration analysis repeated following the redesign.

The above equation is based on the following assumptions related to the structural analysis;

  • [K] and [m] – the stiffness and the mass matrices (system) are constant.
  • linear elastic material is assumed;
  • small deflection theory is assumed and no non-linearities are included;
  • No damping is included;
  • {F} is not present, so no excitation of the structure is assumed.
  • The structure can be unconstrained (rigid body modes present) or partially or fully constrained.

The aim of this write up is illustrate a free vibration analysis through the finite element method, both: without and with pre-stress. The following topics are covered in this article;

Section 2 of this article explains finite element equations for a bar, beam and a plane stress element starting from the corresponding governing differential equation. The finite element equation is arrived at using Gallerkin's weighted residual method. The finite element equations for computation of element stiffness and the element mass matrices are derived. Through the section 2 , it is attempted, firstly, to explain the approach to generate finite element equations and then to get a detailed understanding of the development of the consistent mass matrix (for some of the finite elements) which forms one of the major steps in a dynamic finite element method analysis.

Firstly, the mass matrix formulation for the 1d bar and beam elements is explained based on the Galerkin’s weighted residual approach.

Energy based method for the formulation of the mass matrix is then discussed which is followed by the mass matrix evaluation for a general three dimensional finite element and a 2d bar element using the energy based approach.

Section 3 describes the numerical approach involving the mapping procedure from a parent element to a mapped/general finite element which aids in solving the integrals involving the evaluation of the stiffness, force, and the consistent mass matrix during the FE analysis solution process.

Section 4 lists the steps carried out in a free vibration analysis procedure using finite element analysis.

A description of the various methods/solvers available for carrying out the solution of the Eigen value problem will be presented in a separate article which is under preparation. This part is not covered in this article.

A note has been included in section 5 to point out the physical significance of rigid body modes following a free vibration analysis

A Matlab code has been developed for free vibration analysis through the steps described in section 3 / 4 and the results of Matlab output are presented in section 6 for a few benchmark problems. No commercial software has been used and the results presented in this section are the direct output of the Matlab program for free vibration analysis of structures using bar, beam 2d (plane strain, plane stress), 3d (8 noded hexahedron) finite elements. The element mass matrices, element stiffness matrices and consequently the system mass and stiffness matrices are generated through the Matlab code developed. For the solution of the Eigen value problem the Matlab solver has been used.

Finally in section 7, free vibration with pre-stress analysis is explained. This is significant, because, generally, the stress state of a structure under constant loads (static) may affect its natural frequencies. This can be important especially for structures thin in one or two dimensions. For example, consider a guitar string being tuned: as the axial load is increased (from tightening) the lateral frequencies increase. This is an example of the stress stiffening effect.

5. Significance of rigid body modes following a free vibration analysis

 

It might be worthwhile to include a note at this point on the interpretation of getting rigid body modes following a free vibration analysis.

A rigid body mode can be mathematically identified with zero natural frequency. Rigid body modes are the result of infinite deflection along a particular degree of freedom and are related to the constraints in the finite element model. For instance, if one has a 6 degree of freedom beam element oriented perpendicular to a 5 degree of freedom plate element, then, the beam will want to rotate about its longitudinal axis and the plate won’t be able to stop it (no constraint from the plate element). If you tie a couple of beam elements to the end of your original beam, but orient it in the plane of your plate element, the new beams will constrain the rotation of your original beam and eliminate the rigid body mode.

The significance of a rigid body mode depends entirely on your modelling intent. If you have a static object with no moving parts, a rigid body mode will tell you that you haven't properly constrained your model. If you have a rotating shaft on a piece of machinery and your finite element model calculates 1 rigid body mode, you may have some confidence that the model is performing as expected.

7. Free vibration analysis with pre-stress

In some cases, one may want to consider pre-stress effects when performing a free vibration analysis. This is because; the state of stress of a structure under constant (static) loads may affect its natural frequencies. For example, consider a guitar string being tuned- as axial load is increased (from tightening), the lateral frequencies increase. This is an example of stress stiffening effect.That is; the effect of pre-stress on the system will be to increase the stiffness of the system. Since, w = sqrt(k/m) , pre-stressing will cause an increase in the natural frequencies i.e. the system will oscillate faster, hence the time period will be less.
