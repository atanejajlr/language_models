1. Introduction

This write-up attempts to explain the terminologies 'natural frequency' and 'natural mode of vibration' in the study of structural dynamics. Section 2 of the write up illustrates a free vibration analysis of a single degree of freedom system and revisits the concept of simple harmonic motion, section 3 qualitatively brings about the concept of natural mode of vibration through a simple 2 storey frame. Section 4 briefly explains the importance of natural modes and the reference to my technical bog in this section explains how a vibration analysis problem from the physical coordinate system can be transformed into the modal coordinate system thus uncoupling the equations of motion which forms the basic significance of natural modes.

Contrary to what was observed for the single degree of freedom system (section 2 above), we observe that the motion is not simple harmonic and the frequency cannot be defined. Further, the deflected shape varies with time.

But, it is interesting to note that if the free vibration is initiated by appropriate distribution of displacement in various degrees of freedom, then, an undamped system would undergo simple harmonic motion without change of deflected shape.

Two characteristic deflected shapes exist for the 2 degree of freedom system such that if it is displaced in one of these shapes and released, it will vibrate in simple harmonic motion, maintaining the initial deflected shape. Both the degrees of freedom reach their extreme displacements at the same time and pass through the equilibrium positions at the same time. The point of zero displacement is called a "node". Each characteristic deflected shape is called a "natural mode of vibration"

4. Importance of natural frequencies and natural modes

Mode shapes provide a set of mutually orthogonal base vectors which facilitate decoupling of equations of motion in modal coordinates making it easier to solve. These modal coordinate solutions are then converted back to original physical coordinates by using the same set of mode shapes.

The results of this are utilized to simplify seismic (or, any forced vibration) analysis by using method of mode superposition.
