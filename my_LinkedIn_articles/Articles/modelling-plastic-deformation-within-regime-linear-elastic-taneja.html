1. Introduction

 

This article is the continuation of my series of blogs on “Fracture Mechanics”.

The crack-tip plastic zone:

In my earlier article titled: Analytical approach to the computation of stress and displacement fields in the vicinity of the crack tip (https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/analytical-approach-computation-stress-displacement-fields-taneja?trk=mp-author-card), I had discussed in sufficient detail the stress field solution in an infinite plate with a central crack under biaxial loading. Through the solution, it was observed that a stress singularity existed at the tip of the crack (termed as the “root r” singularity in the fracture mechanics literature). In practice, the materials tend to exhibit a yield stress above which they deform plastically. That is: there is always a region around the tip of the crack in a metal, where plastic deformation occurs and hence in reality, a singularity cannot exist. The plastic region in the fracture mechanics literature is termed as the: crack tip plastic zone.

The blunting of the crack tip:

In metals, as the material deforms plastically, quite often the flow of the material makes the crack tip blunt as seen in the picture below.

Figure: Blunting of the crack tip as a result of plastic deformation 

This blunting of the crack tip in turn reduces the magnitude of the stress components. Thus, many potential catastrophic failures are avoided just by local plastic deformation of the crack tip. Thus, a structural designer in order to avoid fracture failure in the components prefers to have a large plastic zone developed at the crack tip. This is contrary to the likings of the conventional designer who tries hard to avoid yielding at any portion of the component. In a way the size of the plastic zone may be regarded as a parameter in representing the fracture toughness of the material.

In this article, an attempt is made to explain the simplified procedures, to model the plastic zone in the vicinity of the crack tip within the regime of linear elastic fracture mechanics. That is: to find suitable modifications to the analytical expressions involving the computation of stress intensity factor that have been developed using the theory of linear elastic fracture mechanics so that an extensive elastoplastic / non-linear finite element analysis can be avoided.

It should be noted that the approach outlined in this article will be applicable if the plastic zone size is small compared to the plate dimensions and the crack length i.e. material is assumed to exhibit small scale yielding. In such cases, it may be possible to modify the analytical expressions involving the stress intensity factors (evaluated using the theory of LEFM) to account for small scale yielding / limited plastic zone.

Section 2 of this article starts with the approach used estimate the size of the plastic zone along the direction of the crack. The sections starts with the explanation to arrive at the first estimate of plastic zone without accounting for any redistribution forces followed by applying a correction (as explained by Irwin in 1957) to this first estimate, thus, accounting for redistribution of forces as a result of yielding of the material around the crack tip.

Section 3 of this article, explains about the shape of the plastic zone considering Von Mises yield criterion – both in plane stress case and plane strain case by obtaining a polar plot between plastic zone length “r” and angle θ (recall that expressions for stress field around the crack tip are expressed in polar coordinates).

Finally, section 4 defines the expression for the effective crack length aeff . The expression (as explained in section 2) is obtained considering the redistribution of forces and thus the Irwin’s correction to the size of the plastic zone.

3. Shape of the plastic zone

 

 So far, the extent of the plastic zone along the x axis has only been considered – i.e. the emphasis was on the length of the plastic zone. An impresion of the “shape” of the plastic zone may be obtained by examining the yield condition for different angles “θ” starting from 0. That is: we will be plotting rp vs. θ as a polar plot by varying θ and marking the values of “r”. In doing so, a proper yield criterion should be imposed like the Von Mises or the Tresca yield criterion.
