2.  Surface conditions

For many common types of loading, such as bending and torsion, the maximum stress effects occur at the surface. Furthermore, the surface is exposed to harsh treatment such as corrosion or unexpected loads such as scratch or impact. In addition, experiments show that even the failure of axial loading usually begins at the surface.

With all the evidence pointing to the surface, it is necessary to understand the effect of surface and the treatments that improve the surface properties.

Surface treatments:

Since the fatigue life is clearly related to the condition of the surface, many researchers devote their efforts to surface treatments. Generally, treatments that produce compressive residual stresses at the surface positive impact on fatigue life. Some of the surface treatments are discussed below;

1)    Carburizing and Nitriding: Carburizing and Nitriding produce higher strength and hardness at the surface and this improve fatigue life.

It may be noted that nitriding and carburizing are the two most common heat treatment practises for surface hardening functional components. The main difference between the two is that in nitriding, the nitrogen atoms are made to diffuse into the surface of the parts being processed, whereas in carburizing, carbon is used.

2)    Shot peening: Shot peening is a cold working process used to produce compressive stress residual stress layer and modify mechanical properties of metals and composites. It entails impacting a surface with shot (round metallic, glass or ceramic particles) with force sufficient to cause plastic deformation. Thus, shot peening is used to strengthen and relieve stresses in structural components.

It may be noted that shot peening may have negative impact on the corrosion resistance. This is because the surface after shot peening is packed with dimples and does not remain smooth anymore. And, any non-smooth surface is easier to trap moisture, has more surface area exposed, and thus is more vulnerable to corrosion.

3)    Cold working: Cold working is the process that compresses and squeezes the sheet metal between rollers. It improves the accuracy of thickness and surface finish. Cold working produces complex residual stress layer on the surface such that it postpones the crack propagation from the surface and extends the fatigue life.


3.  Thermal fatigue, low temperature and high temperature effects

 Thermal fatigue:

Thermal fatigue can be categorized into thermal loading, high temperature and low temperature fatigue. The mismatch of coefficients of thermal coefficients between 2 mating parts introduces thermal stresses when temperature changes (see for fundamentals on thermal stresses and strains:https://www.mathalino.com/reviewer/mechanics-and-strength-of-materials/thermal-stress.

Another thermal loading case is the component being exposed to heat and cold or back and forth repeatedly. When changes in temperature are severe and / or frequent thermal loading / thermal fatigue; thermal failure sets in.

Low \ temperature effects:

Low temperature often makes the usually ductile material more brittle. The cracks in brittle material propagate more rapidly more than in ductile material which worsens the fatigue life.

6.  Reliability (scatter of test data)

 

Reliability accounts for the scatter of test data. E.g. an 8% standard deviation in test data requires a fatigue strength reduction factor value of 0.868 with 95% reliability and 0.753 for 99.99% reliability.

It should be underscored that the SN(Wohler) plotted values are mean values based on a number of tests resulting in loads at failure. The strength values are mean values implying a 50% survival rate. To enable determination of design strength values with a higher survival rate i.e. 90% upwards then the indicated strength values must be reduced. R.R. Moore Fatigue Testing System

The R.R. Moore Rotating Beam Fatigue Testing System is recognized as the standard for rotating beam fatigue testing for more than 70 years!

Figure 3: The R-R Moore rotating beam fatigue testing machine

The testing system is designed based on the rotating beam principle. The specimen functions as a simple beam symmetrically loaded at two points. When loaded, one half the resolution, the stresses in the fibres originally below the neutral axis are reversed from tension to compression or vice-versa. Upon completing the revolution, the stresses are again reversed so that during one revolution, the test specimen passes through a complete cycle of flexural stresses.

Effect of temperature on inelastic effects (plasticity and creep)

The effect of high temperature on mechanical properties can be associated with transformation of the material structure due to diffusion process, aging, dislocation restructuring (softening) and recrystallization.

In general, such processes imply that plastic deformation can occur more easily at elevated temperature. This can lead to creep which can be defined as continued plastic deformation under sustained load. With respect to fatigue, it can imply that more plastic deformation and creep can occur in the plastic zone of a fatigue crack which may imply to both micro-cracks and macro-cracks. As a result fatigue damage accumulation might be enhanced. During creep under sustained load, creep failures occur by grain boundary sliding, void formation, void growth and coalescence.

Miscellaneous factors kf

Other factors that affect endurance limit may be: residual stresses, corrosion, cyclic frequency, etc.

Residual stresses may either improve the endurance limit or affect it adversely. Generally, if the residual stress on the surface of the part is compressive, the endurance limit is improved. Fatigue failures appear to be tensile failures, or at least caused by tensile stress and so any factors that reduce the tensile stress also reduce the possibility of fatigue failure. Corrosion has adverse effects on endurance limit so if there is a corrosive environment, the endurance limit has to be appropriately reduced. Sometime in-house data is available within companies for such factors.
