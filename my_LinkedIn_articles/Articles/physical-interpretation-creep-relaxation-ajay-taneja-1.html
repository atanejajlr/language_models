Physical interpretation of creep and relaxation

Created on 2015-11-06 23:58

Published on 2015-11-07 00:00

This is a short and a light note attempting to bring about the physical interpretation of creep and relaxation.

Think of the cloth line in which you dry clothes.

In order to make sure it is taut, you pull it and tied it. Let us say the material is nylon cable.

First process: relaxation 

After a few days, do you expect it to be taut ?

(Taut  means tight in tension)

No. After a few days you find that  the tightness has decreased! This is stress relaxation.

Yet, the distance between the two nails you strung the cable remains the same. Or, in other words, the total remains the same.

Second process: creep

On the other hand, the sagging that you see in the same cloth line after a few days of hanging clothes is due to creep because the length of the cable is no more the same now.  So, creep is increase in length--or rise in strain, as you see in the rope line.

And, in the same rope, what is tension after a few days, does it reduce too?

Once it sagged a little bit, the tension is no more ther. Or, there is very less tension! The tension is created only when you hang the clothes.

On the other hand, when you tightened initially, you pulled it hard and tightened to create an initial stress. This gradually vanishes initially when the length of the rope remained constant.

Two processes. One after the other:

The first process is relaxation and the other (after it lost all the tautness) is the creep process. 

Pure relaxation when strain remains a constant...

Pure creep when stress remains a constant...
