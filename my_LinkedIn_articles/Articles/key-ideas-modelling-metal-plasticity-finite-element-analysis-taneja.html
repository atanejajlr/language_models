1. Introduction

For many engineering applications, elastic constitutive equations are sufficient to model the material behaviour since large plastic strains are by and large undesirable and will lead to failure.

There are situations, however, where it of much interest to predict the structural behaviour subjected to loads that will cause the material to cross its elastic limit. Examples of such situations include;

  1. Fan blade off simulation
  2. Design of crash resistant vehicles
  3. Plastic design of structures
  4. Prediction of time dependent effects due to creep and shrinkage in structures
  5. And more…

There are different plastic constitute laws, which are intended to be used in different applications. They all fall under 2 broad classifications;

  • Rate independent plasticity: This is used to model metals deformed at low temperatures deformed at modest strain rates
  • Rate dependent plasticity (or, viscoplasticity): This is used to model high temperature creep and to model metals deformed at high temperatures at high strain rates where the material strength is sensitive to deformation rate (i.e. the material yield stress increases with increase in strain rate).

This article focuses on rate independent plasticity. Following concepts related to the modelling of rate independent plasticity are discussed in this write-up;

 1) The decomposition of strain into elastic and plastic parts,

2) Yield criteria, which predict whether the solid responds elastically or plastically,

3) Strain hardening law, which controls the how the material yield stress increases (or how the material hardens ) with plastic straining,

4) The plastic flow rule, which determines the relationship between stress and the plastic strain under multi-axial loading,

5) The elastic unloading criterion which models the irreversible behaviour of the solid.

Having discussed the above key points concerning the modelling of plasticity for computational analysis in sections 2 through section 6, the essential ingredients of a plasticity algorithm for a finite element analysis are discussed in section 7.

The ideas discussed in sections 2 through section 6 form the basis of developing finite element methods that model rate independent metal plasticity. This will be appreciated as one would read the concluding section on the ingredients of a plasticity algorithm in this article.

2. Decomposition of strain into elastic and plastic parts

One of the first steps in a plasticity algorithm is to find out whether the material during a particular load increment is responding elastically or elasto-plastically.

If the material is not in the elastic range, it is required to compute the plastic strains, the correct stress level during the load step and other parameters. In the paragraphs that follow (in this section) an attempt is made to explain the definitions of elastic and plastic strain and the corresponding math expressions.

Elastic strain:

Some people define (incorrectly!) the elastic strain as: “any strain that takes place before the stress exceeds the yield stress”. However, it is important to note that, for hardening materials, elastic strain is also increasing during post-yield. This is because, with hardening, the material is becoming stronger i.e. its yield stress (I prefer to use the term “elastic limit” than “yield stress”, however, I’ll stick to using “yield stress” as most literature and people do) is increasing, so the elastic strain ought to increase as the material hardens (i.e. even post-yield). Hence, a better definition of elastic strain would be: “the strain that is removed during unloading”.

Plastic strain:

Plastic strain is the permanent strain that remains after unloading. Total strain is the sum of elastic and plastic strains.

3. Yield criterion

A yield criterion is used to mathematically identify when yielding happens. For uniaxial loading, the yield criterion typically includes the current level of stress minus the initial yield stress added to the function which describes the type of hardening.

For multi-axial state of stress, a scalar measure is used to denote an equivalent stress(rather, an index) which can then be compared to the yield stress of the material.

There are many different yield criteria. But, the most common yield criteria generally used in nonlinear material models in finite element analysis software’s is;

i) Von Mises yield criterion

ii) Tresca’s yield criterion

 

3.1 Von Mises yield criterion

 

Physical interpretation of Von Mises yield criterion

Von Mises yield criterion proves extremely useful in cases of multi-axial loading because it provides a means of obtaining a single equivalent scalar measure which can then be compared with the yield stress of the material to predict numerically whether the material is yielding or not (i.e. whether the material is in the elastic or the plastic range).

For a multi-axial loading, a complex 3 dimensional state of stress is developed. The state of stress at any point may be denoted by a stress matrix;

The Von Mises criterion is a formula for calculation whether a state of stress at a point will cause the material to yield or not.

There are three “principal stresses” that can be calculated at any point acting in the x, y, z directions. Von Mises found that, even though none of the principal stresses exceed the yield stress of the material, it is possible that the material starts yielding depending upon the combination of stresses. Von Mises criterion is a formula for combining these 3 stresses into an equivalent stress which can then be compared to the yield stress of the material.

The equivalent stress is often called the “Von Mises stress” – which is not really a stress but a number used as an index.

Physically, the Von Mises yield criterion (as described mathematically in the next sub-section) is arrived based on the assumption / experimental observations that for a multi-axial state of stress, yielding starts when the maximum distortional / shear energy in uni-axial / simple tension.

The yield criteria are based on the following experimental observations;

  • A hydrostatic state of stress (all principal stresses equal) will never cause yield, no matter how large the stress
  • The yield criterion is based on the assumption of isotropic behaviour because they depend on the magnitudes of principal stresses and not their directions.

4. Strain hardening laws

 

Experiments show that if you plastically deform a solid, and then unload it, and to reload it again, the material will start yielding at a stress at which it was unloaded i.e. the resistance to plastic flow will have increased. This is called ‘strain hardening’.

Modelling strain hardening

One may model strain hardening by relating the size and shape of the yield surface to plastic strain in an appropriate way. Described below are two fundamental approaches;

4.1      Isotropic hardening laws

4.2 Kinematic hardening laws

7. Essential ingredients of a plasticity algorithm

 

Plasticity algorithms are often used in finite element analysis programs to model non-linear material behaviour. In such cases, the finite element analysis program is incremental in nature. Each new load increment increases stress levels, strain levels and displacements. During each of these increments the plasticity algorithm is used at the element level in the finite element analysis program.

The following steps are carried out during each increment and in each finite element;

  1. We start with the known stored variables: ε, εp,epsilon_dash^p
  2. An increment of strain at a load step gives, ε = ε + Δ ε
  3. At the beginning of each increment (or load step), it is not known whether the new level of stress will cause yielding (in a particular finite element) or not. For this reason, it is initially assumed that the incremental stress is elastic. In plasticity algorithms, the resulting new level of stress is often termed as the ‘trial stress’.
  4. The trial stress is plugged into the yield condition “f” (section 3) and a trial yield condition ftrial is evaluated. The ftrial equals (σtrial – yield stress).
  5. This yield stress is a function of the equivalent plastic strain ( . See section 4.1 where the yield stress is expressed as a function of the equivalent plastic strain for isotropic hardening laws; see section 4.3 for the yield stress for kinematic hardening laws.
  6. It is then checked whether: ftrial <= 0
  7. If ftrial <= 0, it means that the stress is elastic and thus, σ =σtrial
  8. If ftrial > it means that a plastic step has occurred.
  9. If a plastic step has occurred, them, the problem is to find σ, (the equivalent plastic strain) such that f(σ, α) = 0 . See section 5.
  10. Having this information, the plastic strain at this load step εpis calculated. See section 5.
  11. This completes the computations for a plastic step. Exit the algorithm for the plastic step
  12. Repeat the steps ii through ix for all elements
  13. Repeat the steps i to x for all load increments
