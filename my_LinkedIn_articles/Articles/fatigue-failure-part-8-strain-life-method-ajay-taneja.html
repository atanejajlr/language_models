1.   Introduction

 In my ongoing series of blogs on Fatigue Failure, the emphasis has predominantly been on Stress Life Diagram – the famously termed S – N Diagram, where “S” refers to the applied stress and “N” refers to the number of cycles at each particular stress level. To obtain the diagram; a test specimen will be cyclically loaded to a particular stress level and the number of cycles to fracture recorded.

Besides the stress level method – very briefly defined above and in sufficient detail in the part 2 of my series of blogs [On Fatigue Failure – Part 2: Stress – Life Diagram, Endurance Limit, Approximation of the S – N Curve] : https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/fatigue-failure-part-2-stress-life-diagram-endurance-limit-taneja/; the method that significantly went into researched during the 1960’s was the Strain – Life Method.

It might be recalled that a fatigue failure most often begins at a notch, crack or other areas of stress concentrations. When the stress at the discontinuity exceeds the elastic limit, plastic strain occurs. If the fatigue fracture has to occur, cyclic plastic strains must exist. Thus, one will need to investigate the behaviour of the material under cyclic plastic strains. The strain resistance of the material is a better measure of the fatigue performance than stress resistance.


This article goes into some detail of the Strain – Life Method and is organized as follows;


·       Section 2 of the article gives an insight into the material behaviour under cyclic plastic strains.

·       Section 3 talks of the strain-life testing and the material constants that go into the strain – life relationship. These being: Fatigue Ductility Coefficient, Fatigue Ductility Exponent, Fatigue Strength Coefficient, Fatigue Strength Exponent.

·       Section 4 goes into the mathematics of the Manson – Coffin Relationship between fatigue life and strain.


2.   Material behaviour under cyclic plastic strains

 Johann Bauschinger first discovered that the elastic limit in compression is lower than that in tension when a specimen of iron, subjected to tension over the first elastic limit is loaded in reverse on the compression side. The phenomenon known as “Bauschinger” effect played an important role in the investigation of metal fatigue. More insight into the “Bauschinger effect” can be found in my articles below;

3.   Strain – Life testing


In order to plot strain vs. life, the total strain controlled during the test is divided into an elastic part and a plastic part. The elastic strain is computed as the stress range divided by the elastic modulus. Plastic strain is obtained by subtracting the elastic strain from the total strain.

b)    Fatigue Strength Coefficient: It is the true stress corresponding to the fracture in the first reversal in the stress – strain curve. This is show by the point A in Figure 1. The elastic strain line begins at this point (figure 2)

c)     Fatigue Ductility Exponent: It is the slope of the plastic strain line and is the power to which the life 2N must be raised to be proportional to the true plastic strain amplitude. If the number of stress reversals is “2N”, then “N” is the number of cycles.

d)    Fatigue Strength Exponent: It is the slope of the elastic strain line and is the power to which the life 2N must be raised to be proportional to the true stress amplitude
