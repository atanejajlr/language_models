1. INTRODUCTION

The aim of this write up is to illustrate all the essential elements of the “frequency-domain” method of dynamic analysis for a SDOF system. It is not attempted herein to extensively describe the frequency-domain method but to just present the basic concepts and stages in this method of dynamic analysis.

The “time-domain” method of dynamic analysis involves solving the governing differential equation(s) wherein the loading function is a function of time “t” -p(t)].- In the frequency domain method the loading function, the function p (t) is instead expressed as a linear combination of sines or cosines or (more convenient mathematically) complex exponentials such as exp (iωt). Thus, a problem in time domain “t” becomes then a problem in frequency domain “ω”. This, makes the dynamic problem much easier to solve numerically.

The various stages in the frequency-domain method of analysis could be briefly described as below;

i. The first stage -as evident from the lines stated above, is to convert the applied loading expression from time domain to the frequency domain form.

ii. In the second stage, having expressed the periodic loading as a series of harmonic terms, the response to each of the individual harmonic loading is determined.

iii. In the third stage, the total periodic response of the undamped structure is expressed as the sum of the individual responses obtained in stage ii.

The frequency domain method is limited to those systems for which the governing differential equation is linear. It is this linearity (also known as the principle of superposition) which allows us to combine the individual responses in step iii above.

It may be noted that the analysis through frequency-domain method could include any type of loading: periodic or non-periodic. However, the discussion in this write-up, is deliberately restricted only to periodic loading to demonstrate the basic ingredients of the frequency-domain method than to rigorously get into extensive details about this method.

The content of this write-up is organized as below;

Section 2 of this write-up describes how an arbitrary periodic loading of period Tp can be conveniently expressed in a Fourier series form with harmonic loading components at discrete values of frequency.

Section 3 expresses the exponential form of Fourier series.

Section 4 describes the response of linear system to Fourier series loading i.e. the response to individual harmonic terms / loading components at discrete frequency values.

Section 5 concerns the total periodic response of the undamped structure then can be expressed as the sum of the individual responses to the loading terms.

5. SUMMARY OF THE STEPS IN FREQUENCY DOMAIN METHOD OF ANALYSIS

A summary of the steps in carrying out dynamic analysis in the frequency-domain can be described as below;

1. The first stage wherein the Fourier coefficients of periodic loading are evaluated, may be looked upon as the conversion of the applied loading expression from the time-domain to the frequency-domain form. In other words, the values pm = p™ which express the applied load at a sequence of time, tm, are replaced by complex terms Pn = P(i) which express the harmonic load amplitudes at specified sequence of frequencies, . These values constitute the frequency domain expression for the loading.

2. In the second stage of analysis, the single degree of freedom response for a given frequency is characterised by the complex frequency response coefficient, Hn, which expresses the harmonic response amplitude due to a unit harmonic loading at the frequency. When the response coefficient is multiplied by the complex Fourier series coefficient Pn that expresses the harmonic input amplitude at that frequency, the result is the complex response amplitude Vn, for that frequency. Thus, the complete set of values Vn for all the considered frequencies, constitute the frequency­domain expression of system response.It should be notes that the frequency response coefficient will be complex in case of a damped response but will be real in case of undamped response.

3. In the final stage of the analysis, the frequency­domain response is converted back to the time domain by superposing the response components determined for all of the frequencies included in the Fourier series loading expression. In this superposition operation, it is necessary to evaluate all of the response harmonics at the same instants of time, tm, recognizing the relative phase relationships associated with each frequency. When these response harmonics are added together, the final result is the time­domain expression of the response history, vm = v(tm).

The negative frequencies do not represent anything with a clear physical interpretation. It is best to think of exp(iw) as a vector rotating counter-clockwise in the complex plane, and exp(iw) = exp(-w) as a vector rotating clockwise. Adding them cancels the imaginary part, giving cosine, and subtracting them cancels the real part, giving i*sin. The negative frequencies are present in order to make the final answer real instead of complex, because they allow you to combine the complex exponentials in to sin and cos terms.
