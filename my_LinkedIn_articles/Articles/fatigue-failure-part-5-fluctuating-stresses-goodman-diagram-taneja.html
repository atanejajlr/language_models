2.   What are Fluctuating Stresses?

 It was mentioned above that in case of fully reversible stresses, the amplitude of stress always remains the same and the man / average stress is always 0. In case of fluctuating stress, one has the stress pattern as shown in the figure below;

Figure 2: Fluctuating Stresses

As it may be noticed from the Figure 2, one cycles from 1 unit of stress to 0.5 units and then back from 0.5 unit to 1 and so on and so forth.

The above pattern is going to repeat through all cycles, thus there are no varying stresses present – but the pattern is no longer fully reversible, thus, as there are no varying stresses present and the R-Ratio ≠ -1.

The question now remains how to quantify fluctuating stresses – just like fully reversible stresses could be quantified in the absence of stress data (as re-iterated in section 1 above). This is discussed in the subsequent section.

4.   Stress concentration for Mid-Range and Alternating Stresses

 The above stresses σa and σm – the alternating and the mid-range stresses which will be used in the quantification of the fluctuating stresses are equal to the nominal stresses induced by the loads Fa and Fm respectively in the absence of notch / stress concentrations.

In the presence of notch and stress concentration, the alternating and the mid-range stresses will have to be multiplied by the fatigue stress concentration factor Kf in which case the mid-range and the alternating stresses will take the form: Kfσao and Kfσmo induced by loads Fa and Fm respectively as long as the material remains in the elastic limit without yielding.

When plastic strain occurs around the notch due to localized yielding the material properties Sy and Sult are modified due to strain strengthening (strain hardening) and there are slightly different measures adopted to quantify the fluctuating stresses. These are not discussed in this blog.

The next section focusses on how these parameters: alternating and mid-range stresses are employd in Goodman diagram and modified Goodman equation to determine the remaining part of the life if the part lies in the finite life region.

5.   Goodman (Haig) Diagram

The Goodman (Haig) Diagram is drawn by plotting the alternating stress on the y-axis and the mid-range stress on the x axis. Let us first say that we have a component that is experiencing only mid-range stress and no alternating stress. That means: the component is only experiencing static loading and no time varying loading. Thus, if we plot stress vs time we get the figure below;

Figure 5: Component subjected to static loading (no alternating stress)

In the above scenario, the part will fail under static loading- thus at strength of ultimate tensile strength of the material, that is: Sut.

Thus under 0 alternating stress, drawing a graph between mid-range and alternating stress, Sult can be pointed on the graph as below;

Figure 6: Component subjected to static loading (no alternating stress) will fail at Sult (the ultimate strength of the material)

Now consider the scenario, wherein the part is experiencing only alternating stress and no mid-range stress. That is: the part is experiencing a fully reversible loading as below, then, the part will have infinite life as long as the part is stresses below the endurance limit.

Figure 7: Component subjected to alternating stress only ( will have infinite life as long it stays below the endurance limit)


Thus, if we plot a straight line as below from the ultimate strength Sut to the endurance limit Se, we get the figure below. This forms a triangle with corner as the origin of the graph. Any combination of the mean and alternating stress within the triangle is considered safe i.e. the component will have infinite life whilst the combination outside the triangle will have finite life.

Modified Goodman Line:

The straight line from the Sut to Se (ultimate strength to the endurance limit) is termed as the “Modified Goodman Line”.

Figure 8: Component subjected to alternating stress only ( will have infinite life as long it stays below the endurance limit)

The equation of the Modified Goodman Line can be written as;

If n < 1, then the component lies in the finite life region.

If n > 1, the component lies in the infinite life region.


It can be seen that the modified Goodman equation tells you whether the component is in the infinite life region or in the finite life region. The Goodman Diagram / modified Goodman equation does not calculate the number of cycles to failure if the component is in the finite life region. More analysis is required to ascertain the number of cycles to failure if the component is in the finite life region. This is illustrated through the example evaluation 2 as covered in section 7.
