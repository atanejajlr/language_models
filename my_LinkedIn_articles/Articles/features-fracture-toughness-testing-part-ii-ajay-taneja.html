2.   Fundamental differences between plane strain fracture toughness testing and plane stress fracture toughness testing:

Unlike, plane strain test specimens, the specimens used for plane stress fracture toughness testing need not be always fatigue cracked. In case of a plane stress fracture toughness test, the flaw may be introduced into the specimen in the form of a saw cut (i.e. by cutting out a slit in the specimen). Then, using the geometric dimensions of the specimen, the length of the crack and stress required to fracture the specimen, the fracture toughness can be principally calculated.

It should be noted that if the fracture of the plane stress specimen is not preceded by slow crack growth, the simulation of the crack by means of a saw cut is not permissible. This can be explained on the basis of energy balanced concept. The energy balance concept was discussed in one of earlier blog in this series [https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/extension-griffiths-analysis-ajay-taneja?trk=mp-reader-card] and is repeated with a view to explain the physical reasoning for a slow crack growth during a plane stress fracture toughness test.


Energy balance concept:

I had pointed out in the above article that in a plane stress condition (thin plates), that the resistance “R” to crack growth does not remain constant and increases as the crack grows. The increase in resistance with increase with crack growth is attributed to the effect of plastic zone i.e. the resistance to crack growth increase as the material “flows”/ “plasticizes”.

It was also elaborated in the article that a “flaw” / “crack” in a component (or, a “thin” plate) will start to grow when an energy equal to the resistance “R” – the surface energy of the material can be delivered to form 2 new surfaces above and below the plane of crack;

Figure: Energy contribution to crack growth


This resistance “R” is different for different materials and in case of ductile materials increases as the material plasticizes.


Recalling the figure below, which I had elaborately discussed in this blog;

Figure: ‘R’ curve in case of thin plates (plane stress case)

Following could be interpreted from the above figure;

i.         At stress level, σ1, the resistance “R” offered by the specimen (note: I do not use the word ‘material’ but use ‘specimen’), exceeds the energy release rate “G”


ii.         At stress level, σ2, the energy release rate “G” equals the resistance “R” and the cracks starts growing – this is the initiation of the fracture process (or, perhaps the ‘stable’ fracture process) before catastrophic failure.


iii.         It is noticed that as stress level σi increases, the resistance “R” increases which is the result of the plastic deformation (this may be imagined as analogous to strain hardening).


iv.         Thus, as the stress level is increased, the crack continues to grow but will not lead to a catastrophic failure until a stage is reached where;

v.         It may be thus be stated that the necessary and sufficient conditions for fracture may be taken as;


a)     G = R


That is, a stable fracture is initiated when the energy release rate “G” equals the resistance “R”.

and       b)


    This condition implies that if the slope of “G” equals “R”, a catastrophic failure occurs.

Ensuring a slow crack growth:

If the crack is sharp enough i.e. the “saw cut” simulated crack is sharp enough, slow crack growth begins when G = R and catastrophic failure occurs when;

The process in this case is that of a stable fracture.

Whereas, saw cuts can be made so blunt that a crack growth does not occur until a stress σ3 larger than σc. In that case no energy balance can be reached and immediate fracture instability occurs, not preceded by slow growth. This might imply, that the saw cut induced crack has already caused plastic deformation of the material (which is demarcated by the bluntness of the crack), thus resulting in immediate catastrophic failure not preceded by a stable fracture / slow crack growth – as shown in the figure below;


Figure: Blunt crack tips in plane stress testing


The permissible bluntness depends upon the material properties; sharper cracks are required for materials with lower toughness.


What is the critical crack length?

The question that arises is whether one will report ‘a1’ as the critical crack length or (a1 + Δa1) as the critical crack length. It should be noted that ideally, the critical crack length is “a1” but due to the process of stable fracture, it has extended to a1 to Δa1 – the extension from a1 to Δa1 is not due to any growth mechanism like fatigue or stress corrosion cracking.

Growth of crack by cutting a slit

The question sometimes arises whether it is permissible to establish the critical crack size by cutting a slit while the specimen is under constant load. Tests have shown that this could result in an overestimation of the critical crack length.

It was stated in the aforementioned paragraph that if “sawing” is continued beyond the stress associated with the critical crack length and then the specimen loaded, a catastrophic failure is seen without the occurrence of a stable fracture. It was also mentioned that the “bluntness” of the crack may be the reason attributed to this behaviour but it has been argued through tests that bluntness of the crack cannot be regarded as the sole cause of the discrepancy. Thus, there is another recommendation that to always grow the crack by loading than to first by sawing to the point where slow crack growth initiation / stable fracture was about to occur.

3.   Anti - buckling guides

Providing anti-buckling guide in thin panels – for plane stress fracture toughness testing

To start the discussion in this topic, let us first consider the problem of a plate with a hole subjected to uniaxial tension as shown in the figure below;

Figure: Plate with a hole in under uniaxial tension

It can be derived that, at r = a,

σθθ = σxx (1 – 2 cos θ)


The stress variation around the hole can be plotted as below;

Figure: Variation of stresses around the crack tip

It can be clearly seen that the normal stress on the boundary of the hole varies from 3σ to –σ and is 0 at four points on the boundary. Point to be noted here is that along the x-axis, the stress level is minus (–) of the far field stress i.e. compression. This can be mathematically seen through the expression:

σθθ = σxx (1 – 2 cos θ)

At θ = 0;

σθθ = – σxx

Thus, even though the load applied is uniaxial tension (along x- axis), there exists compressive stresses at the boundary of the hole along x axis. Similar observation is seen in case of a crack. Because of compressive stresses developed, buckling of the panel is possible since it is relatively thin in plane stress conditions. From the plane fracture toughness testing point of view, it may be questioned if this buckling is desirable or not and whether it should be prevented during a plane stress fracture toughness test.

This is actually a debatable point. In thin panels, crack buckling can occur if the load is increased. If this (buckling) is to be avoided, anti-buckling guides, as shown in the figure below are provided:

Figure: Crack buckling and anti-buckling guides


Thus, one point of view is to prevent the crack buckling during plane stress fracture toughness testing by providing anti-buckling guides. Another point of view is that in actual / real structure, the buckling might not be fully restrained. Thus, the result without the anti-buckling guides is preferred in many instances.

Feddersen’s concept of apparent toughness:

In case of thin panels (as in aerospace structures) , one needs to access KIc data for a variety of thicknesses. For panels wide enough to produce the real KIC, the relationship between the residual strength and crack length was illustrated in the figure in the preceding section

The residual strength for smaller crack lengths cannot actually be got through the figure above because for low values of crack length, the figure shows residual strength greater than the yield stress of the material. In the region where the residual strength exceeds the yield stress of the material, the specimen will exhibit an apparent toughness less than that of KIC.

Feddersen proposed an engineering approach to determine the residual strength of the component in region of lower crack length where the fracture strength – in the above diagram appears greater than the yield stress of the material.

Considering the graph between – the crack length and the residual strength of the component, Feddersen proposed that 2 straight / linear tangents to the idealized curve may be drawn;

o  the first tangent drawn from the point σ = σys, where σys is the yield strength of the material

o  another tangent drawn from the point “W” on the axis – where “W” is the width of the specimen.

This is illustrated in the figure below:

Thus, for crack lengths between 2a1 and 2a2, the residual strength is determined by the curved portion and for crack lengths less than 2a1 and between 2a2 and W, the residual strength is determined by the straight line portion of the above diagram.

It should be noted that the straight line portions do not have any physical basis and are just a design measure proposed by Feddersen.
