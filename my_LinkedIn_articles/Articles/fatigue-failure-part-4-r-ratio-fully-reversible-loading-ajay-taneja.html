2.   R – Ratio

 State of stress for generating a stress-life diagram:

 In one of my earlier blogs on Fatigue Failure (https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/fatigue-failure-part-2-stress-life-diagram-endurance-limit-taneja/), I had pointed out that a material’s fatigue performance can be characterized by an S-N diagram; where ‘S’ refers to the applied stress and ‘N’ refers to the number of cycles at each particular stress level. Thus, for proper fatigue characterization, it is necessary to obtain the data in a test such that the loading during the test mimics the cyclic loading that the part will be experiencing in application.

 Thus, if the part will be experiencing uniaxial tension, the test data will be generated in uniaxial tension. If the part is experiencing shear/torsional stress or bending stress, the test will involve torsional loading or will involve a bending test (discussed here: https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/fatigue-failure-part-3-factors-affecting-strength-ajay-taneja/?trackingId=RH3EEQwtLWL%2F3vNLCsRb5g%3D%3D).

 R – Ratio during the test

 R – Ratio is simply the ration of minimum stress experienced during a cycle to the maximum stress experienced during the cycle. Stress values may be positive (tensile) or negative (compressive).

 R – Ratio (denoted by ‘R’) may be 0,-1 or between -1 and = +1 depending upon whether the loading is unidirectional tension or involves tension – compression cycles or involves fluctuating stresses. That is;

a)    Unidirectional, R = 0 testing: If the test specimen starts out unloaded (zero stress) and then is loaded to maximum positive required stress level and unloaded to the next cycle, then, R – ratio is exactly 0. This is called R = 0 testing.

 b)    Fully reversible stress, R = -1, testing: The most extreme case would be if each cycle consisted of loading the test specimen to a given stress level and then loading it to an equal and opposite stress level in the other direction. In the case where the minimum stress is negative of the maximum stress, then R – ratio will be exactly -1. This is known as fully reversible ( R = -1) loading / testing.

c)    Fluctuating stresses ( R – ratio lies between +1 and 0 or between -1 and 0): In other cases, the part may be pre-loaded to a certain stress level that might never be removed during the life of the part. The part may be loaded to the maximum stress level and then released back to the pre-load. In this case, the minimum value will be some small positive value associated with the pre-load and maximum value will be a larger positive value. If the pre-load is a positive value R – ratio will be greater than 0 and less than 1.

 IT is also possible to have a minimum compressive stress of lower magnitude as a preload and then load the specimen to a maximum positive value. In this case, the R – ratio will be between -1 and 0.

3.   Revisiting – Stress Concentrations

The developments of the basic stress (or, equilibrium / compatibility) equations for a state of stress like tension, torsion, compression or bending, it is always assumed that no geometric irregularities occurred in the member under consideration. But, it is quite difficult (or, rather impossible!) to design a component without permitting some changes in cross section of the members.

Any discontinuity in a part alters the stress distribution in the neighbourhood of the discontinuity so that the elementary stress equations no longer apply to the state of stress in the part at such locations. Such discontinuities are called “stress raisers” and the regions in which they occur are called “stress concentrations”.

Stress concentrations can also arise from some irregularity not inherent in the member, such as, tool marks, scratches, holes, notches, grooves, etc.

 Stress concentration factors in static loading

In static loading in ductile materials, the stress concentration factor is not usually applied because the plastic deformation at the notch root results in notch blunting due to yielding at the notch root thus reducing notch root radius.
