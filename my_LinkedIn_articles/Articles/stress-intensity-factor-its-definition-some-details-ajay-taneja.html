1. Introduction

 

This is the continuation of my series of blogs on Fracture Mechanics.

A crack front exists within a material like a line running from one region into another. The vicinity of the crack tip offers interesting information as the magnitudes of the stresses at the crack tip are very high [this can be physically felt by recalling that when one wants to break a wooden stick by bending it, we make a notch. The notch creates high stresses which in turn make the notch tip move easily].

Knowing the stress or displacement field in the vicinity of the crack tip is useful in several ways:

  • A material scientist may devise ways to develop new materials which can diffuse high stresses at the crack tip.
  • A designer may modify some features such as notches to minimize stresses
  • An experimentalist can think of methods of characterizing cracks by measuring stresses and strains near the crack tip. One of the biggest advantages of stress analysis near the crack tip is that it leads to define a very important parameter called ‘stress intensity factor’ to characterize a crack. In comparison to the energy release rate, stress intensity factor is more simpler for the designer and easier for laboratory experiments so as to determine the material properties

 

This is a brief write up, without going into the extensive mathematics that leads into the definition of stress intensity factor, its expressions considering an infinitely long plate for mode I, mode II and mode III loading.

Section 2 of this article lays emphasizes on the formal definition of Linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) and section 3 brings out the mathematical expressions for stress and displacement fields for a rectangular plate of infinite dimensions with a central crack.

Section 4 goes into the definition, details of stress intensity factor considering the expressions illustrated in section 3. Whereas, section 3 and 4 consider a plane strain case for a mode I loading, section 5 lists the stress and displacement fields for an infinitely long plate for mode II and mode III loading written in terms of the stress intensity factor KII and KIII respectively.

 

2. Analysis of cracked solids and linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM)

 

 

Brittle materials remain elastic even in the vicinity of the crack tip. The development of stress and displacement fields – is in many applications confined to only brittle materials. It should be underscored that, whilst most of the engineering materials are ductile, by confining the study to brittle material (thus, elastic analysis), one can obtain closed form solutions to many problems. Also: in many cases, the material may be ductile but the size of the plastic zone is small so that accounting for the plastic zone might be many times unwarranted.

Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics (LEFM):

This leads to a very important sub-field in Structural Mechanics: Linear Elastic Fracture Mechanics wherein only and elastic analysis is carried out to determine the stress and displacement fields in cracked solids near the crack tip and to characterize a very important parameter called: stress intensity factor (SIF) which is used to describe a crack.

4. Stress intensity factor

 

It may be observed through the expressions of the stress tensor components that, for a given geometry there are 2 main variables:

  • far field stress ‘σ’
  • crack length ‘a’
