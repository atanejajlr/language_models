1. INTRODUCTION

There are fundamentally two approaches that may be followed to compute the dynamic response of structures:

  • Time domain approach
  • Frequency domain approach

The “time-domain involves solving the governing differential equation(s) wherein the loading function is a function of time “t” : p(t) and the in frequency domain approach the loading is discretised into a series of harmonic components (sines and cosines) by Fourier series / Fourier transformation and the dynamic response is expressed by superimposing the responses due to each harmonic component.

Most common dynamic analysis procedures that may be classified under the time domain approach include: Duhamel / convolution integral based procedure wherein the loading p(t) is considered to be a succession of short-duration impulses, and the free vibration response to each impulse becomes a separate contribution to the response history at any subsequent time.

Another very common (or “most” familiar) dynamic analysis procedure based on the time domain approach is the mode superposition (or modal) analysis procedure. The aim of this article to present in details the various steps involved in the mode superposition analysis and explain the main concept of the mode superposition analysis.

Section 2 of this article presents the equation of motion of a simple 2 storey frame and reveals how these equations are coupled i.e. the stiffness matrix is non-diagonal. The essence of the mode superposition method is to uncouple these equations of motion which can be done by transforming the problem from the physical coordinate system to another coordinate system.

Section 3.1 and section 3.2 clearly explain that the transformation matrix could be well chosen as the mode shapes obtained by solving the eigen value free vibration problem wherein the all the degrees of freedom of the structure oscillate simple harmonically with the same frequency.

Section 3.3 explains the how the orthogonality properties of these mode shapes (each mode shape being an eigen vector) helps in uncoupling the equations of motion.

Section 4 explains how the equations of motion could be solved once the transformation to the normal coordinate system is carried out and finally the section 5 summarizes the mode superposition analysis fundamentally.

It may be noted that the equations are coupled through the stiffness matrix i.e. the stiffness matrix is non-diagonal and hence one equation cannot be solved independent of the other and the equations must be solved simultaneously.

One would normally resort to the numerical approximation procedures like the explicit central difference method or the implicit Newmark’s integration scheme in order to solve the coupled equations.

A simpler method to obtain the dynamic response of the frame is the mode superposition method which principally transforms the equations of motion from the physical coordinate system into another co-ordinate system known as the normal or the modal coordinate system and in this coordinate system the structural matrices i.e. the mass and the stiffness matrix are diagonal.

Therefore, the solution of the simultaneous coupled equations is completely avoided in this coordinate system and would solve one equation independent of the other. The solution of each equation now (after the problem is transformed into the normal coordinate system) becomes equivalent to solving an equation of a SDOF system. That is: instead of solving a coupled system with ‘N’ degrees of freedom in the physical coordinate system, one, instead solves ‘N’ uncoupled equations wherein each of the ‘N’ equations is independent of the other and thus solved as an equation of a SDOF system.

The next section explains how it is possible to obtain a set of ‘N’ uncoupled equations from ‘N’ coupled equations by transforming the problem from the physical coordinate system to another coordinate system where all the structural matrices are diagonal. This coordinate system where the structural matrices are diagonal is called the “normal” coordinate system or the “modal” coordinate system.

5. SUMMARY: MODE SUPERPOSITION ANALYSIS

From the above write-up it can be summarized that;

i. Equations of motion for MDOF systems are generally coupled.

ii. Coupling between co-ordinates is manifest in the form of structural matrices being non-diagonal

iii. Coupling is not an intrinsic property of a vibrating system. It is dependent upon the co-ordinate system. This choice itself arbitrary.

iv. Equation of motions are not unique. They depend upon the choice of the co-ordinate system.

v. Normal modes of vibration (mode shapes) are special undamped free vibration solutions such that the degrees of freedom of the structure oscillate simple harmonically at the same frequency with the ratio of the displacements between any two degrees of freedom being independent of time.

vi. The frequencies at which normal mode oscillations are possible are called the natural frequencies.

vii. The modal matrix (each column of the modal matrix corresponds to a mode shape) is orthogonal to the mass and the stiffness matrix.

viii. The undamped normal modes in conjunction with proportional damping models (damping is not discussed in this write-up) simplify vibration analysis considerably.
