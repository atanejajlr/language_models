1.    INTRODUCTION

 

This article is the continuation of my series of blogs on "Fracture Mechanics".

Fatigue crack growth:

Many structures are subjected to varying / cyclic loading with amplitude so low that failure due to yielding or crack growth was never to be expected. However, experience tells a different story! After a large number of load reversals, structural parts fail unexpectedly often leading to overall collapse of the structure. The phenomenon is a result of “fatigue crack growth”.

In structures which have developed cracks as a result of fatigue loading, the designer is often posed with the following questions:

  1. Is it safe to operate the component or the machine? This is a very important question from the safety point of view
  2. Then, if it is safe to operate the component / machine – for how long?
  3. Thus, is it possible to monitor crack growth, so that, one can disregard the component or stop the machine before catastrophic failure can occur?

 

In order to answer the above questions, one needs to go beyond the conventional fatigue test which gives adequate information relating to the S-N curves and the endurance limit. This forms the section 2 of the article which gives the details pertaining to: the lacuna of the conventional fatigue test.

Section 3 speaks of experimentally obtaining the crack growth curves for a test specimen in order to answer the questions stated above and then section 4 leads to the ever popular Paris Law which can be used to predict the residual life of the structure following crack growth.

The complete life cycle of the crack can be explained through sigmoidal curve and this is discussed in considerable detail in section 5 emphasizing on crack initiation, propagation and catastrophic failure. Whereas, Paris law can be used to model crack propagation phase of crack growth, there are other models which can be used to model the complete sigmoidal curve and this forms the topic of discussion of section 6.

I have planned to discuss the topics on crack initiation and life estimation in 2 parts. The next part of this blog will focus on concepts related to crack closure, effect of overload on crack growth and software tools available with different crack propagation models.

2.    FATIGUE TEST AND ITS LACUNA

 

Considering the simple tension test for a test specimen under uniaxial loading, it may be recalled that the results of a simple tension test could be well used for multi-axial loading in formulating the yield criteria.

In a simple fatigue test, the material performance is characterized by an S-N curve which is a graph of magnitude of a cyclic stress (S) against the logarithmic scale of cycles to failure (N) as shown below.

Figure: S-N curve for brittle Aluminium with an UTS of 320 MPa.

Now, considering the S-N curve as above (from a fatigue test) we were able to derive the concept of endurance limit which gives us the essential information of the cyclic stress permitted to a life of 107 cycles or more. Through the fatigue test one knows that for a given stress level, what is the expected life and that was the only information one can extract from the fatigue test.

Supposing, there is a crack during the application of the cyclic load and one wants to estimate the remaining life then this information cannot be extrapolated through the fatigue test (thus, the fatigue test is not 'as clever' as our simple tension test !) . Thus, the conventional fatigue test is not able to answer the questions as described in the introduction section of this article. In such cases, a more exhaustive test needs to be done to answer these questions.

3.    CRACK GROWTH CURVES

 

In order to answer the above questions, one of the standard specimens for fracture testing is fatigue loaded and the crack growth is monitored at intervals by NDT methods. The fatigue loading is not fully reversed as in the conventional fatigue test but the lower load is kept at zero during the test.

The experiment is repeated at various loads and a plot of crack length vs. number of cycles is obtained for a single material as shown in the figure below.

Figure: Crack growth curves for a specimen at various load levels

 

It can be seen from the graph of the crack growth curves that for the highest load level of 44.4 kN, the crack has grown to a large extent within a few cycles. For smaller loads, the crack takes a longer time to grow. Thus, different crack growth curves are obtained for different load levels

Essentially, one will be collecting voluminous data for a single material wherein you keep changing the load amplitude and for each load amplitude you have a different crack growth curve.

 

4.    PARIS LAW

 

It can be understood through the crack growth curves that crack growth is a function of cyclical load as well as the crack length. And the next challenge now is to be able to use such a data in the design calculations. Hence, the data needs to be normalized (like the stress vs. strain data from a load deflection data) in some form from the crack growth curves of a single material.

The credit for this goes to Paris who was able to fit all the data from the crack growth curves of a single material. The data can be fitted onto a log-log plot choosing x-axis as ΔK which is the stress intensity factor range and y axis as the crack growth rate denoted by da / dN

note: ΔK is the stress intensity factor range which is the difference between the stress intensity factor at maximum and minimum loading

Figure: Experimental data fitted using equation of the Paris Law

It can be seen that all the data from the crack growth curves in the preceding plot is easily fitted through the Paris law.

Thus, the Paris law relates the stress intensity factor range to sub-critical crack growth under a fatigue stress regime. It is one of the most popular fatigue crack growth model used in materials science and fracture mechanics. In the expression,

It should be noted that the crack growth model provided by Paris is purely empirical and does not have a theoretical basis to the above equation. It is actually a curve fitting exercise using voluminous amount of data that has been collected. The Paris law intelligently brings together the effect of applied stress and the crack length on the crack growth rate through a single parameter ΔK. The role of environment was not considered in the Paris law and correction to the Paris law to incorporate the role of environment was carried out by several researchers later and is considered later in this article.

5.    LIFE CYCLE OF A CRACK

 

Crack initiation and catastrophic failure

Thus, from the above discussion, it can be concluded that, once the crack growth has begun (or, more precisely the crack has started propagating), it is analytically possible to calculate the remaining life using the Paris’ crack growth model.

Before the crack growth phase, there is also the crack initiation phase. It is well known that many ferrous materials exhibit unlimited fatigue life provided that the stress remains within a certain limit. This indicates that very short cracks do not propagate for small values of ΔK. The smallest value of ΔK below which crack propagation does not begin is known as the “threshold stress intensity factor ΔK

The basic idea is, very short cracks may not propagate at all for small values of ΔK. This is one end of the spectrum. At the other end of the spectrum, if ΔK becomes comparable to the fracture toughness of the material; the crack growth rate becomes very large and catastrophic failure becomes imminent. In between, crack initiation and crack propagation is the crack growth phase.

Thus, the complete life history of crack can be described in 3 phases:

  • crack initiation phase
  • crack growth phase
  • catastrophic failure

Sigmoidal curve

As stated above, the entire life cycle of a crack can be divided into 3 regions:

  • crack initiation phase (Region I)
  • crack growth phase   (Region II)

 

  • catastrophic failure   (Region III)

The 3 regions could be represented on a curve termed as: sigmoidal curve (termed so because of the shape of the curve) as shown below;

Region I:
