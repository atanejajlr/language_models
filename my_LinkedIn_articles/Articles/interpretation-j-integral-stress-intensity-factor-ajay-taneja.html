3.   Need for elasto-plastic fracture mechanics


Linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) can be usefully applied as long as the plastic zone is small compared to the crack size. This is usually the case in materials where fracture occurs at stress appreciably below the yield stress and under conditions of plane strain. In such conditions fracture can be characterized by K1C or G1C.


When plane stress conditions prevail, the crack tip plastic zone is larger than in the case of plane strain. If, however, the plastic zone is larger compared to the crack size (high fracture stress and / or high crack resistance) linear elastic fracture mechanics does not apply.

The problem (of the non – applicability of LEFM) occurs in two common scenarios;


o  Occurs in low fracture toughness materials with very short cracks

o  Occurs in materials with high toughness

4.   J – as stress intensity factor

As mentioned in my preceding article [https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/j-integral-ajay-taneja ], the specialty of J is that it is comparable to “G” the energy release rate in case of linear elastic fracture mechanics (LEFM) as well as the stress intensity factor K. The preceding article dealt with the interpretation of “J” as the energy release rate “G” whereas the current article will deal with is interpretation as “J”.

Hutchinson, Rice and Rosengreen:

Hutchinson, Rice and Rosengreen independently showed that J characterizes crack tip condition in case of a non-linear / elasto-plastic material just as “K” characterizes crack tip condition in a linear elastic material. They showed that in order to remain path independent, the product of stress and strain must vary as 1/r near the crack tip. Thus, this type of singularity in case of elasto-plastic fracture mechanics was different from that in case of linear elastic fracture mechanics. 7.   Observations: LEFM vs. EPFM

Following points should be recognized;

·       Once the crack tip blunts as a result of plastic flow; there exists: a fracture process zone which is engulfed by the HRR field which is the “J” dominated zone and the stresses in this zone are dictated by the expression in section 6 (ii). The HRR field is surrounded by the K field wherein the stresses are determined using LEFM approach which is then followed by the general stress field.


  • Both LEFM and EPFM (HRR field) approached infinite stresses at the crack tip as “r” approaches 0 which is not true in reality. In reality, the crack tip will blunt as a result of plastic flow and this blunting of the crack tip is not accounted for either in LEFM or EPFM (HRR field).


  • When n = 1 (expression in section 6 ii); ‘n’ being the strain hardening index; square root singularity is seen for the stress field – n = 1 being the acse of LEFM. So, this is an indirect verification that the value of stress as predicted by Hutchinson – Rice and Rosengreen is indeed correct.
  • For n = ∞, the material model represents a perfectly plastic case as shown in the figure below;


Figure: Perfectly plastic material behaviour


  • At distances upto the order of twice the crack tip opening displacement from the crack tip, HRR field is not valid.


  • In highly plastic materials; “J” can replace “K” in the fracture criterion – J based fracture mechanics is analogous to K based fracture mechanics; both J and K represent the strength of the stress field.
  • Practical application of J based fracture mechanics is much more involved than K based fracture mechanics. This is because the result of J also depends upon the stress strain curve of the material; hence tabulating expressions of J as for K (see section 2) is difficult. A sophisticated nonlinear analysis with the Ramberg – Osgood material model (or, equivalent) has to be carried out to get the value of J.


  • Similar to KC, the fracture toughness of the material obtained based on LEFM, one needs to evaluate JC the fracture toughness of the material based on elasto plastic fracture mechanics.


  • It is often noticed that although people work in the domain of linear elastic fracture mechanics; they compute “J” as illustrated in the preceding blog and use the identity to find out K (remember: J = G in case of brittle materials).


  • Since EPFM is computationally costly, many people do not get involved in it. Instead; as said above “J” is often used to evaluate K in case of linear elastic fracture mechanics

8.   More details on crack tip opening displacement

There have been different approaches provided by Irwin (and Keiss and smith) and Wills / Cotrell with regards to crack tip opening displacement (CTOD). 

Firstly; Irwin, Keiss and Smith in 1958, in order to broaden to the applicability of linear elastic fracture mechanics proposed a plasticity enhanced stress intensity factor in which crack lengths were enhanced suitably.

Then; Will and Cotrell in 1969 advanced an alternative concept that would even apply beyond general yielding conditions. Wells evaluated CTOD /COD using Irwin’s plastic zone to estimate for the problem of a central crack in an infinite strip.

Irwin’s definition of crack tip opening displacement:

I had discussed in one of my earlier blogs on Fracture mechanics: “Modelling of plastic deformation within the regime of linear elastic fracture mechanics” [https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/modelling-plastic-deformation-within-regime-linear-elastic-taneja?trk=mp-reader-card] about Irwin’s model for accounting plastic deformation – the model is based on the approach of introducing an enhanced fictitious crack length in order to account the plastic deformation. This is based on the premise that when the materials begin to flow plastically; there is loss of stiffness and hence the crack length will be larger than that of in LEFM.

The term crack tip opening displacement appears to be a misnomer in reality because if there is a crack tip; then physically there cannot be a displacement of the tip ! The terminology “crack tip opening displacement” comes from the plasticity corrected fictitious crack tip as illustrated in the figure below:

Figure: CTOD as per Irwin’s model incorporating effect of plasticity

 

The crack opens up as shown in the figure above and the opening of the crack as a result of plasticity as marked above is known as: “crack tip opening displacement”.

Tracy’s definition of crack tip opening displacement

As discussed in section 5 – the HRR field and more in section 6 wherein the zones around the crack tip are expressed relative to CTOD, crack tip opening displacement was obtained by drawing mutually perpendicular lines at angles 45 degrees- cutting the blunted crack tip; the height being taken as CTOD.

In practice, CTOD is measured experimentally and ASTM E 1820 has standard procedures for reporting CTOD.
