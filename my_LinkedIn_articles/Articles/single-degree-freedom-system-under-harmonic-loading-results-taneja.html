1. INTRODUCTION

This write up attempts to explain the results and interpretation of single degree of freedom system under harmonic excitation. It should be noted that understanding the response to harmonic excitations provides an insight on how a system will respond to other types of forces. This is true because any arbitrary loading function can be expressed as a sum of sine and cosine functions by Fourier series/ transform, thus,the results of a harmonic excitation are useful for all practical purposes. Also: harmonic excitations are encountered engineering systems such as force due to unbalanced rotating machinery and has several useful applications in earthquake engineering.

Section 2 of this write-up presents the mathematical steps involved in solving the governing differential equation of a SDOF system subjected to harmonic force. Though this section appears comprising of complex mathematical equations and terminologies, it lays emphasis on the physical interpretation of terms like: complimentary factor and particular integral which are often not explained in text books. IT is also attempted to explain the interpretation of each term involved in the response equation.

Section 3 explains the inferences one may draw through the solution of the governing differential equation of SDOF subjected to harmonic excitation.

2.6 Interpretation of the solution.

The equation (the complete solution) in section 2.4 contains two distinct vibration components;

1. the sin ωt term which gives the oscillation at the forcing or exciting frequency

2. the sin ω_nt and cos ω_nt terms giving the oscillation at the natural frequency of the system

The first of these is the forced vibration or the steady state vibration for it is present because of the applied force no matter what the initial conditions. The latter is the transient vibration which depends on the initial displacements and velocity. It exists even if u (0) = 0 and (0) = 0

In the figure in section 2.5, the transient component is shown as the difference between the solid and and dashed lines where it is seen to continue forever. This is only an academic result because in real systems damping is present which makes the free vibration decay with time and for this reason the component is termed as ‘transient component’

3. INFERENCES FROM THE OF RESPONSE OF HARMONIC LOADING

3.1 Deformation or the displacement response factor

The deformation or the displacement response factor is the ratio of the amplitude of the dynamic (or vibratory) deformation to the static deformation.

The figure below shows the deformation response factor plotted as a function of ω / ω.

Figure: Frequency ratio vs. deformation response factor

Following inferences may be drawn from the above diagram;

  • When ω / ωn is small (i.e. the force is “slowly varying”) the deformation response factor is only slightly greater than 1 i.e. the amplitude of vibration is essentially the same as static deformation.
  • When ω / ωn > √2 Rd <1 i.e. the deformation amplitude is less than the static deformation.
  • As ω / ωn increases beyond √2, Rd becomes smaller and approaches zero as ω / ωn ->∞

The above statements indicate;

a) Very fast excitations produce nearly no deformation, while very slow ones are nearly static.

b) Near resonance, the deformation can be very large, and it is here that oscillating loads can cause mechanical failure of the material (usually metal fatigue because the large deformations stretch the material beyond its elastic limit, so that permanent plastic deformation occurs). Note that when systems have many degrees of freedom, they will have many natural frequencies, so that resonances are possible at many different forcing frequencies. Excessive deformation is then much more difficult to avoid.

c) People who carry out vibration analysis of large freight trucks experience during major part of their job calculating the many natural frequencies of the truck body, comparing them to the forcing frequencies (which come from bumps in the road as the truck passes over them), and recommend changes to the truck design which will move the natural frequencies of the truck far away from the forcing frequencies.
