2.  Crack closure

Elber (1970) came out with the concept of “crack closure”. Elber observed experimentally that a fatigue crack growing under cyclic tension can close on itself at about half the maximum load as shown in the figure below:

              Figure: Early crack closure and reduction in ΔK to ΔKeff

 

2.1  Experimental observation of opening and closing of crack

In order to experimentally ascertain whether a crack is closed or open, Elber explained that the load – deflection curve of a cracked body experiencing crack closure is bi-linear.

For a crack to open, the slope of the load deflection curve changes – initially the material is more stiff i.e. the initial slope is dictated by the closed crack (more stiff) and then the slope changes indicating an open crack. So, a fatigue crack on account of its opening and closing will experience a load deflection which is bilinear and this was first brought about by Elber.

 

Figure: Load deflection curve of a cracked body experiencing opening and  closing of crack

 

2.2 Categories of crack closure

There are various categories of crack closure:

o  Plasticity induced crack closure: Consider a sinusoidal loading as shown in the figure below.

                                         Figure: Sinusoidal loading

As the load is increased from 0 to the peak load, there will exist some plastic deformation at the crack tip. Thus, there will be a plastic zone surrounded by a much larger elastic zone as shown in the figure below:

                              Figure: Plastic and elastic region at point B

Once the load is reduced from the point B to the point C, the elastic region will spring back and will tend to compress the plastic zone thus introducing a compressive load on the plastically deformed portion. This will result in compressive strains and compressive stresses around the crack tip.

After several cycles, the crack grows in length and if the crack grows in length the plastic zone also grows in length as shown in the figure below:

Figure: Increase in size of plastic zone with increase in number of cycles of load

As the size of the plastic zone increases, so will the compressive strains around the crack tip. Elber postulated that it is these residual compressive strains and compressive stresses that cause the closure of the crack tip much before the zero load in the cycle.

In view of these residual compressive strains, again, the crack faces do not open until K reaches Kop as shown in the figure below:

                      Figure: Effective stress intensity factor

Where,

ΔKeff = Kmax – Kcl

And Kcl is the stress intensity factor at which the crack closes.

o  Wedge induced crack closure

Oxide induced closure:

Once people understood the concept of crack closure postulated by Elber, several other mechanisms have been identified. One was the plasticity induced crack closure as discussed above and then there was wedge induced crack closure.

This is usually associated with aggressive environment – the corrosion products become wedged between crack faces as shown in the figure below;

Figure: Oxide induced crack closure (corrosion products wedged between crack faces)

As thee load is reduced, it appears, because of the corrosion products that the crack is closed. It has the similar effect of crack closure as discussed above causing the effective stress intensity factor to be: ΔKeff = Kmax – Kcl

3.   Effect of overload on crack growth and retardation of crack growth rate

An overload is a pulse (or a set of pulses) of higher amplitude on a constant amplitude fatigue load as shown in the figure below:

Figure: An overload pulse on a the constant amplitude fatigue load

The crack propagation rate retards considerably after the overload pulse. The retardation of the crack propagation rate is a result of the influence of the plastic zone. During the unloading portion of the overload pulse, the surrounding elastic material tries to regain its original state. But, the plastic zone cannot regain its original shape, therefore, compressive residual stresses are developed at the vicinity of the crack tip.

In the follow up pulses of the constant amplitude loading, the tensile loading at the crack tip is suppressed by the residual compressive stress. Consequently, da/dN is lowered appreciably. The crack tip may remain within the enclave of residual compressive stress for many subsequent pulses of constant amplitude load as shown below. This concept is elaborated in more detail below.

Figure: Enclave of residual compressive stresses developed by the overload and plastic zones of subsequent pulses

It therefore takes some load cycles for the crack tip to come out of the enclave of the compressive stresses and it is then only then that da / dN regains its original value based on the length of the crack and ΔK of that location.

The above can be more evident through the figure below which shows how three single overloads increase the crack growth life by almost a factor 5 (comparing figures A and B below):

It is evident from the figure above that after a period of very slow growth (immediately following the overload); gradually the original growth rates are resumed. viii. Thus, as a result of the residual compressive stress, the crack growth retards during the subsequent cycles of loading and the normal growth is resumed once the “residual” stresses have been overcome.

Residual compressive stresses during overloading

If an overload occurs, a much larger plastic zone is formed resulting in still larger compressive stresses as shown in the figure below;

 

             Figure: Residual stresses during an overload

 Thus, after an overload, a much extensive residual stress system is present than one before the overload. This – more extensive system – acting against the applied stress causes subsequent crack growth to be retarded. Once the crack has grown through the over load stress field, normal crack growth is resumed. Thus, the residual compressive stresses in case of overload is obviously larger and the beneficial effect of retardation of crack growth is more pronounced.
